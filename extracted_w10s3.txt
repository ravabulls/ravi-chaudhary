2.  RFID — Radio Frequency Identification Tags
What is RFID?
Tags with embedded chips that store information and relay it via radio frequency signals to an RFID reader
Key difference from barcode/QR: no close proximity or precise angle needed — reader can be remote
Product can be embedded deep inside a location and still be scanned
Product can be moving — reader captures signal as it passes through the reader zone

Key Advantages Over Barcode / QR
No line-of-sight required — tag can be inside a sealed container
Scan-on-the-move — no stopping required (critical for conveyor lines)
Bulk scanning — scan a whole container or pallet without opening it
No manual intervention for reading — gates open automatically once tag is identified

Applications
Manufacturing / assembly lines: items move on conveyor — scanned automatically at each workstation to ensure correct routing
Containers & pallets: import/export — scan container as it moves through port zone without stopping
Toll collection (FASTag — India): vehicle RFID tag triggers automatic toll debit and gate opening — zero manual intervention
Vehicle fleet tracking: embedded tag in vehicle tracks movement through checkpoints
Pet / animal tracking: tag embedded under skin — scanned without harming the animal; useful when travelling or pet goes missing

3.  Wireless Sensors & IoT — Real-Time Monitoring
What are Wireless Sensors?
Devices that capture product / environment conditions continuously in real time (not just at scan points)
Connected to IoT (Internet of Things) platforms — data streamed via internet to cloud or local storage
Unlike barcode/QR/RFID which give snapshots, sensors give a continuous live feed

What Data Can Sensors Capture?
Climatic conditions: temperature, humidity, pressure — critical for pharma, food, blood, IV fluids
Location: GPS-based position tracking as product moves
Vehicle status (Telematics): speed, fuel, wear & tear, vibration, breakdown alerts — especially for remote routes
Product must be within the sensor zone to capture relevant data — placement matters

Telematics
Definition: tracking vehicle movement parameters — speed, fuel, route, vibration, vehicle health — using sensors
Products being transported in the vehicle are monitored indirectly via the vehicle's environment
Especially useful for remote routes with poor roads where vibration can damage products

Applications
Cold chain management: medicines, drugs, IV fluids, blood — real-time temperature must stay within range
Food & agriculture: meat, vegetables, dairy — freshness monitoring during long-haul transport
High-value cargo: any shipment where condition during transit determines usability at destination

★  Wireless sensors = only technology that gives CONTINUOUS real-time data. All others (barcode, QR, RFID) give point-in-time data at scan events.

5.  Levels of Product Labelling — Packaging Hierarchy
Tags and codes can be applied at different levels of packaging — choice depends on what granularity of tracking is needed

Packaging Level
What it Is
Typical Info Captured
Technology Used
Primary (Item level)
The product itself — e.g., a bottle, box, blister pack
Individual unit serial no., batch, expiry, price
Barcode (most common), RFID chip embedded
Secondary (Carton level)
Multiple units packed in a carton / inner box
Carton code, contents description, quantity
QR code, barcode on carton face
Tertiary (Pallet level)
Many cartons stacked on a wooden pallet for B2B transport
Pallet ID, contents, origin, destination
QR code, RFID tag (no need to unpack to scan)
Container / Vehicle
Container on ship/truck, or the vehicle itself
Container ID, vessel/vehicle no., route, timestamp
RFID (passive/active), telematics sensors on vehicle

★  Rule: track at the level you want to manage. B2B bulk shipments → pallet-level. Retail consumer transactions → item-level (primary pack).

6.  Product Tracing — Connecting Timestamps Across the SC
The power of T&T: timestamp data at every scan point creates a complete product trace — a full chronological history
This trace can be used to root-cause quality issues or diagnose service delays

Walkthrough — Consumer Complaint Scenario
Customer in Market 1 reports quality issue with a product they purchased. T&T enables backward trace:

1 (start)
Consumer complaint at retail
Customer reports quality issue. T&T reference: checkout scan at this retail point → identifies product code + timestamp
2
Retail → Warehouse
Product code traced to carton scan at warehouse/DC dispatch. Identifies which pallet it left on and when.
3
Warehouse → Factory
Pallet scan at factory dispatch + arrival scan at warehouse. Reveals transit time and any delays.
4
Factory — batch/line
Carton scan at packaging/dispatch from factory → linked to production batch + date → reveals manufacturing context
5 (end)
Factory → Suppliers
If manufacturing is not the issue — trace further upstream to raw material arrival records at factory → reveals procurement delays or material issues

Result: know the exact batch, factory, transit path, and timing — determine whether it's a manufacturing, transport, or storage issue
Same logic applies forward: diagnose stockout delays in Market 2 by tracing dispatch timing at each node

★  Trace = backward reconstruction using timestamps. Every scan event is a node in the product's history. Missing scans = gaps in traceability.

7.  Process Design — Key Questions Before Implementing T&T
Technology alone is not enough — process design determines what to track, when, how, and for whom

Process Design Question
What to Consider
Scope of monitoring?
End-to-end SC or only certain stages? Depends on the problem you are solving (e.g., delivery delays = warehouse to retail only; product quality = all the way to supplier)
Data of interest?
Movement only? Or also health (temperature, humidity)? Connects to application — determines choice of technology
Parallel or sequential?
Is scanning a separate dedicated activity or can it happen alongside another process (e.g., product on conveyor → auto-scanned)?
Transmission & storage?
On-premise servers or cloud? Which database/ERP system? How does data integrate with existing IT?
At which stages captured?
GRN at receipt? Regular cycle counts? Pre-dispatch? Real-time in-transit? Determine scan checkpoints per scope
Manual or automated?
If labelling/scanning hampers productivity → automate (RFID, sensors). Depends on volume, cost-benefit
Commercial returns?
T&T takes time to stabilize. Set clear milestones — when to expect ROI. Do financial analysis before committing
Energy requirements?
Technology (sensors, RFID) needs power — battery or electricity. Factor into operational cost calculation
Documentation linkage?
How does scan data feed into GRN, ASN (Advance Ship Notice), invoices, audit trails?

8.  People — Training & Change Management
What Personnel Need to Be Trained On
Labelling: how to apply codes correctly, label position must be accessible for scanning, information encoded must be accurate
Scanning: skill requirement is low for routine scan-and-load tasks; higher for billing/inventory contexts
Data management: storing records correctly, maintaining database integrity, not tampering with data
Decision-making from data: interpreting dashboards, using tracking data to review processes and flag issues

Automation Reduces Training Need
RFID + sensors can automate scanning — no person needed at scan point
Amazon Go example: customer picks item → product scanned automatically → billed via app on exit → no cashier, no checkout
Monitoring systems can trigger control actions automatically — e.g., sensor detects temperature spike → cooling system activates without human intervention
This is the shift from monitoring-only to monitoring + control systems

Change Management
Migrating from paper-based to automated T&T requires a mindset shift — not just a tool change
Personnel whose job was manual tracking may face job displacement concerns
Action: re-skill displaced personnel — redeploy to higher-value tasks (data analysis, decision support)
Poor change management = implementation failure even if technology is good
Regular training programs + clear communication of job security plans are essential

Session Summary
RFID: embedded chip, remote read, scan-on-move, no line-of-sight — conveyors, containers, tolls, pet tracking
Wireless sensors + IoT: real-time continuous data stream — temperature, humidity, GPS, telematics — cold chain, pharma, food
Telematics: vehicle movement monitoring — speed, fuel, vibration, route — indirect product health via vehicle environment
Cost order: barcode < QR < RFID < wireless sensors. Higher cost = richer real-time data.
Packaging levels: primary (item) → secondary (carton) → tertiary (pallet) → container/vehicle. Label at the level you manage.
Product trace: backward timestamp chain — complaint at retail → retail → WH → factory → supplier
Process design: 9 key questions — scope, data type, parallel/sequential, storage, checkpoints, automation, ROI, energy, documentation
People: train on labelling, scanning, data mgmt, decisions. Automate where possible. Manage change carefully.
Benefits: authentic data, visibility, productivity tracking, collaboration, contracts, monitoring + control
Challenges: high cost, data storage investment, change management, IT integration, technology redesign disruption
