## DuPont Model — Adapted for Operations

<Badge text="Core Framework" variant="note" />

<Aside type="note">
**What is the DuPont Model?** A popular financial analysis framework used to decompose and derive performance indicators from underlying metrics.

Used here in **adapted form** — not the exact original DuPont formulation.

**Central metric:** Return on Assets (ROA)
</Aside>

### Core Formula

<Aside type="note">
**ROA = Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover Ratio**

Both components are **derived indicators** — calculated through mathematical formulas from other measurable metrics, rather than being directly measured themselves. The goal is to maximise both simultaneously to maximise ROA.
</Aside>

## Part 1 — Net Profit Margin

<Badge text="Derived Indicator 1" variant="note" />

### Definition & Formula

<Aside type="note">
**Net Profit Margin = Net Profit / Sales**

A ratio expressing how much profit is earned as a proportion of sales. Can range from less than 1% to over 100% — higher is always better for the firm.
</Aside>

### Building Up to Net Profit

#### Sales

<Aside type="note">
**Sales = Price per unit × Volume (units sold)**

*Example: Price = ₹100, Units sold = 10,000 → Sales = ₹10,00,000*
</Aside>

#### Net Profit

<Aside type="note">
**Net Profit = Sales − COGS − SGA − Interest Expenses − Taxes − …**

All deductions represent obligations the firm owes to external entities. Each one reduces the profit available to the firm.
</Aside>

---

### Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)

All costs **directly related** to producing and distributing the product:

| Cost Component | Description |
|---|---|
| **Cost of Purchase** | Amount paid to vendors for raw materials and inputs |
| **Labour Cost** | Workers directly involved in manufacturing — packaging, cleaning, equipment setup, material movement, and shop-floor activities |

<Aside type="note">
COGS is directly linked to the **activity of creating and delivering the product**. If the activity does not happen, the cost is not incurred.
</Aside>

---

### SGA Expenses — Selling, General & Administrative

Costs **not directly linked** to manufacturing the product:

| Category | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Selling** | Advertisement and promotion costs |
| **General & Administrative** | IT staff, infrastructure management, customer relations, non-shop-floor employees |

<Aside type="note">
A key distinction: wages paid to shop-floor workers who physically produce the product = **COGS**. Wages paid to office staff, IT teams, and admin personnel = **SGA**.
</Aside>

---

### Other Deductions from Profit

| Deduction | Owed To |
|---|---|
| **Interest Expenses** | Banks — cost of borrowing capital |
| **Taxes** | Government — income tax on profits |

<Aside type="note">
**★ Who the firm owes money to:**
- Vendors → via COGS (raw material purchases)
- Employees → via COGS (shop floor) and SGA (admin)
- Banks → via Interest Expenses
- Government → via Taxes

All of these reduce Net Profit and therefore compress Net Profit Margin.
</Aside>

---

### Operations Strategy Levers for Net Profit Margin

| Lever | Financial Impact |
|---|---|
| Reduce cost of raw material inputs through procurement negotiation | ↓ COGS |
| Reduce labour cost through process efficiency or automation | ↓ COGS |
| Reduce advertising and distribution costs | ↓ SGA |
| Choose tax-advantaged manufacturing locations (e.g., Special Economic Zones) | ↓ Tax liability |
| Increase sales volume or price point | ↑ Revenue base → improves margin denominator |

## Part 2 — Asset Turnover Ratio

<Badge text="Derived Indicator 2" variant="note" />

### Definition & Formula

<Aside type="note">
**Asset Turnover Ratio = Sales / Total Assets**

A ratio expressing how much sales revenue is generated per unit of assets owned by the firm. Higher is better — it means assets are being utilised efficiently to generate revenue.

**Goal:** Hold just enough assets to generate high sales — no idle or excess assets sitting on the balance sheet.
</Aside>

---

### Total Assets = Fixed Assets + Current Assets

#### Fixed Assets

Physical assets used **to produce or deliver** the product — they are not themselves sold as the product:

| Type | Examples |
|---|---|
| Manufacturing infrastructure | Plants, factories, production equipment |
| Logistics infrastructure | Fleet of vehicles, warehouses owned by the firm |

<Aside type="note">
Fixed assets are part of **capital expenditure** — expensive both to acquire and to dispose of. They are not easily liquidated; they take time to purchase and sell. They support production and delivery but do not directly or quickly convert to cash.
</Aside>

---

#### Current Assets

Assets that can be **quickly liquidated** (converted to cash) — typically within the financial year:

| Current Asset Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Inventory** | Finished goods, work-in-process (WIP), and raw materials. *Examples: A shampoo bottle = finished goods; dyes, chemicals, plastic = WIP.* Can be sold in the market to generate cash. |
| **Receivables** | Money owed to the firm by customers who purchased on credit. More common in B2B settings than B2C — businesses routinely operate on credit terms. Can be liquidated and treated as a current asset. |
| **Cash & Other Current Assets** | Already fully liquid — immediately available to finance operations. Used for: paying wages, paying small vendors, and covering day-to-day expenses. |

<Aside type="note">
**★ The distinction that matters for operations:**
- **Current assets** = keep operations running day-to-day
- **Fixed assets** = enable production and revenue generation

Both are necessary — but excess of either is operationally and financially inefficient. Excess inventory ties up cash; excess fixed assets reduce asset turnover.
</Aside>

---

### Operations Strategy Levers for Asset Turnover Ratio

| Lever | Financial Impact |
|---|---|
| Reduce excess inventory through lean practices | ↓ Current assets → ↑ turnover ratio |
| Increase inventory turnover — move stock out to consumers faster | ↑ Sales per unit of inventory held |
| Improve cash-to-cash cycle speed — accelerate how fast cash is regenerated from operations | ↑ Liquidity and asset efficiency |
| Maximise equipment and machinery utilisation | ↑ Output per unit of fixed asset |
| Outsource capital-intensive activities to 3PL / contract manufacturers | ↓ Fixed asset ownership → ↑ turnover ratio |

---

## Critical Insight — Levers Are Interconnected, Not Independent

<Aside type="note">
Improving one financial lever **always impacts others** — they cannot be optimised in isolation. Three important interaction examples:

| Action | Intended Effect | Unintended Interaction |
|---|---|---|
| Increase advertising spend | ↑ Sales → may improve margin | ↑ SGA → compresses margin; net effect uncertain |
| Reduce inventory aggressively | ↓ Current assets → ↑ turnover | Risk of stockouts → ↓ sales → hurts both margin and turnover |
| Outsource logistics (3PL) | ↓ Fixed assets → ↑ turnover | ↑ Operational expenses (3PL fees) → ↑ COGS or SGA |

Operations strategy must be designed with **systemic awareness** of all these interactions — not by optimising a single lever.
</Aside>

<Aside type="tip">
**★ ROA is just one indicator.** Do not optimise for ROA alone. Other financial indicators matter too — quick ratio, current ratio, ROCE, inventory days, and more. Design strategy by looking at multiple financial indicators together.
</Aside>

## Industry Examples — SC Decisions Traced to ROA Impact

### Example 1 — Electric Vehicles for Last-Mile Delivery

<Badge text="Impact: ↑ Net Profit Margin" variant="note" />

<Aside type="tip">
**Context:** Same-day and 10-minute delivery services carry high operational costs — fuel, manpower, and multiple trips. In India, approximately 50%+ of total logistics cost is transportation cost, predominantly fuel-dependent road transport.

**SC Decision:** Adopt Electric Vehicles (EVs) for last-mile delivery, charged via renewable energy.

**Financial trace:**
- EV operating cost significantly lower than fuel-based motorcycles
- Not dependent on conventional fuel price volatility
- **↓ COGS / operating cost → ↑ Net Profit Margin → ↑ ROA**

*Seen in: Food delivery and grocery delivery sectors across India.*
</Aside>

---

### Example 2 — Asset-Light Model via 3PL

<Badge text="Impact: ↑ Asset Turnover Ratio" variant="note" />

<Aside type="tip">
**Context:** High logistics cost in India makes it difficult for individual firms to own and manage a full delivery fleet economically.

**SC Decision:** Outsource logistics to 3PL (Third-Party Logistics) providers.

**How 3PL creates value:**
1. 3PL aggregates volumes from multiple client firms → achieves economies of scale
2. 3PL further outsources transport to vehicle owners → no asset ownership at 3PL level
3. 3PL passes cost savings back to the client through bulk volume negotiation

**Financial trace:**
- Firm removes owned vehicles from its balance sheet (no fixed assets)
- **↓ Fixed Assets → ↑ Asset Turnover Ratio → ↑ ROA**

This is the **asset-light model** — using productive assets without owning them.
</Aside>

---

### Example 3 — Digital Transformation: Automating Information Processing

<Badge text="Impact: ↑ Net Profit Margin" variant="note" />

<Aside type="tip">
**Context:** Manual order processing, documentation, and email management wastes time and increases administrative cost — inflating SGA expenses.

**SC Decisions adopted:**
- **Email automation:** reduces manual interaction for routine supplier and customer communications
- **E-commerce order management systems:** sellers and buyers negotiate and transact without manual intervention at every step

**Financial trace:**
- **↓ SGA / admin expenses → ↑ Net Profit Margin → ↑ ROA**
</Aside>

---

### Example 4 — UX-Driven Inventory Turnover in E-Commerce

<Badge text="Impact: ↑ Asset Turnover Ratio" variant="note" />

<Aside type="tip">
**Context:** E-commerce platforms must move inventory out of warehouses and fulfilment centres as quickly as possible to avoid holding costs and free up space for new stock.

**SC Decision:** Invest in clever website and app UX design to increase consumer footfall and speed of purchase.

**Mechanism:** Better navigation → consumer finds product quickly → selects → pays → exits quickly or buys more → inventory liquidated faster.

**Financial trace:**
- **↑ Sales** from improved conversion rates
- **↓ Inventory held** at any given time
- **↑ Asset Turnover Ratio → ↑ ROA**

This example shows that even **digital design decisions** — traditionally seen as a marketing or UX function — have direct, traceable financial performance implications in the SC context.
</Aside>

## Session Summary

| Component | Formula | Operations Levers |
|---|---|---|
| **Net Profit Margin** | Net Profit / Sales | ↓ Input costs (COGS), ↓ Admin costs (SGA), ↓ Tax via SEZ, ↑ Volume/Price |
| **Asset Turnover Ratio** | Sales / Total Assets | ↓ Excess inventory, ↑ Equipment utilisation, Outsource fixed assets via 3PL |
| **ROA** | Net Profit Margin × Asset Turnover Ratio | Optimise both components — but always account for lever interactions |

### Industry Example → DuPont Impact Map

| SC Decision | DuPont Impact |
|---|---|
| EV adoption for last-mile delivery | ↓ Fuel cost (COGS) → ↑ Net Profit Margin |
| 3PL outsourcing (asset-light) | ↓ Fixed assets → ↑ Asset Turnover Ratio |
| Digital / email automation | ↓ SGA admin cost → ↑ Net Profit Margin |
| E-commerce UX improvement | ↑ Sales + ↓ Inventory → ↑ Asset Turnover Ratio |

<Aside type="tip">
**★ Exam Tip:** If asked how operations strategy connects to financial performance — use the DuPont framework as your answer structure.

**Trace any SC decision through to ROA in three steps:**
1. Identify which cost or asset category the decision affects (COGS / SGA / Fixed Assets / Current Assets)
2. State the direction of change in Net Profit Margin or Asset Turnover Ratio
3. Conclude with the effect on ROA

*Example: "Adopting 3PL removes owned vehicle assets from the balance sheet, reducing Total Assets. With Sales held constant, Asset Turnover Ratio improves, which directly increases ROA."*
</Aside>
