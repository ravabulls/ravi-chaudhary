## What is Inventory Segmentation?

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** Categorising a company's inventory into distinct groups based on defined criteria, so that each group receives a tailored management strategy.
</Aside>

**Key benefits:**

| Benefit | Description |
|---|---|
| **Cost optimisation** | Allocate SC resources precisely where they generate the most value |
| **Improved service levels** | Set and maintain the right service level per SKU category |
| **Right storage system** | Assign appropriate storage infrastructure per inventory type |
| **Better picking strategy** | Design warehouse and DC picking processes based on movement frequency and item characteristics |
| **Operational performance** | Overall improvement across fulfilment, replenishment, and inventory accuracy |

## Popular Inventory Segmentation Methods — 4 Traditional Approaches

<Aside type="note">
All four traditional methods are **single-criterion** approaches. While each is powerful within its domain, real business decisions require multiple criteria simultaneously. Single-criterion methods risk oversimplification and suboptimal strategies — which is why advanced multi-criteria methods exist.
</Aside>

### 1. ABC Analysis — Revenue-Based

<Badge text="Criterion: Revenue Contribution" variant="note" />

Classification based on the **revenue contribution** of each inventory item — the Pareto principle applied to inventory.

| Segment | Share of Products | Share of Revenue | Management Priority |
|---|---|---|---|
| **A** | ~20% | ~80% | Highest — tightly monitored, frequent review |
| **B** | ~30% | ~15% | Moderate — regular review |
| **C** | ~50% | ~5% | Lowest — simplified, automated management |

<Aside type="note">
**★ ABC = Pareto principle applied to inventory.** 20% of SKUs drive 80% of revenue — focus management effort accordingly.
</Aside>

---

### 2. FSN Analysis — Movement-Based

<Badge text="Criterion: Speed of Movement" variant="note" />

Classification based on the **consumption rate and speed of movement** through the warehouse.

| Segment | Share of Items | Movement Behaviour | Avg Cumulative Stay |
|---|---|---|---|
| **F — Fast Moving** | ~10% | Very short stay in warehouse; consumed rapidly | Less than 10% of average cumulative stay |
| **S — Slow Moving** | ~30% | Moderate movement; stays longer in warehouse | ~20% of average cumulative stay |
| **N — Non-Moving** | ~50% | Stagnant inventory — no consumption for extended period; inventory turnover ratio < 1 | High cumulative stay |

<Aside type="tip">
**Application of Concepts:** Non-Moving (N) items are a direct holding cost liability. Identifying them allows the business to decide whether to liquidate, discount, or discontinue — rather than continuing to pay storage costs on stock that will never move.
</Aside>

---

### 3. VED Analysis — Criticality-Based

<Badge text="Criterion: Operational Criticality" variant="note" />

Classification based on the **criticality of each item to business operations** — not its value or movement speed.

| Segment | Meaning | Description |
|---|---|---|
| **V — Vital** | Cannot operate without it | Absolutely crucial — business halts if unavailable |
| **E — Essential** | Very important | High priority after Vital; significant disruption if unavailable |
| **D — Desirable** | Not strictly necessary | Operations can continue without it, but quality or efficiency suffers |

<Aside type="note">
**★ VED is most common in manufacturing, hospitals, and defence** — wherever certain items are mission-critical regardless of their cost or demand frequency. A low-cost item can still be Vital.
</Aside>

---

### 4. XYZ Analysis — Demand Variability-Based

<Badge text="Criterion: Demand Predictability" variant="note" />

Classification based on the **predictability of demand** over time — complementary to ABC analysis.

| Segment | Demand Behaviour | Management Approach |
|---|---|---|
| **X** | Little or no variation — highly predictable | Lean inventory; tight replenishment cycles |
| **Y** | Unsteady demand — but can be predicted to a certain extent | Moderate safety stock; regular review |
| **Z** | Very high variation — no discernible trend or causal factors | High safety stock; frequent monitoring; hardest to manage |

---

### Quick Reference — 4 Traditional Methods

| Method | Classification Criterion | Segments |
|---|---|---|
| **ABC** | Revenue contribution | A (high) / B (medium) / C (low) |
| **FSN** | Speed of movement / consumption rate | F (fast) / S (slow) / N (non-moving) |
| **VED** | Criticality to operations | V (vital) / E (essential) / D (desirable) |
| **XYZ** | Demand predictability / variability | X (stable) / Y (variable) / Z (unpredictable) |

## Advanced Inventory Segmentation Approaches

<Badge text="Multi-Criteria Methods" variant="caution" />

<Aside type="note">
Enabled by: the combination of **data availability** and **increased computational power** — both now accessible to most organisations. Advanced methods handle two or more criteria simultaneously for realistic, business-grade segmentation.
</Aside>

### 1. Mathematical Programming

- **Linear Programming (LP)** or **Non-Linear Programming (NLP)**
- Formulates the segmentation problem as a mathematical optimisation model incorporating multiple criteria simultaneously

### 2. Metaheuristics

Used when the problem is too complex for exact mathematical programming:

| Algorithm | Type |
|---|---|
| **Genetic Algorithm (GA)** | Evolutionary optimisation |
| **Particle Swarm Optimisation (PSO)** | Swarm intelligence |
| **Simulated Annealing** | Probabilistic local search |

### 3. AI / Machine Learning

Heavily data-driven — well suited to today's data-rich SC environment:

| Algorithm | Type |
|---|---|
| **Artificial Neural Networks (ANN)** | Deep learning |
| **Support Vector Machines (SVM)** | Supervised classification |
| **Back Propagation Networks** | Neural network training |
| **K-Nearest Neighbor (KNN)** | Instance-based learning |
| **Regression models** | Predictive modelling |

### 4. Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM)

Incorporates **expert opinion** to weigh multiple factors:

| Method | Description |
|---|---|
| **AHP — Analytical Hierarchy Process** | Pairwise comparison of criteria to derive priority weights |
| **Fuzzy AHP** | AHP with fuzzy logic to handle uncertainty in expert judgement |
| **ANP — Analytical Network Process** | Extension of AHP allowing interdependencies between criteria |

### 5. Hybrid Approaches

Combinations of the above — e.g., AHP + ML, GA + LP — used when no single method is adequate alone.

<Aside type="tip">
**★ Exam tip — Advanced methods:** Know all four traditional methods in full detail. For advanced methods, be able to name each category and state *why* it is used: handling multiple criteria simultaneously, accommodating data availability, or managing complexity beyond single-criterion approaches.
</Aside>

## AHP — Analytical Hierarchy Process

<Badge text="MCDM Framework" variant="note" />

<Aside type="note">
**Proposed by:** Thomas L. Saaty

**Type:** Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM) method

**Applications:** Supplier selection, procurement decisions, product and service design, resource allocation, and inventory segmentation.
</Aside>

### Core Idea

AHP organises a complex, multi-factor decision into a **hierarchical structure**, performs **pairwise comparisons** of all factors to determine their relative importance, and outputs **quantitative, consistent priority weights** for each factor. Inputs can come from a single expert or aggregated from multiple experts.

### AHP Hierarchy Structure

![AHP Hierarchical Structure Diagram — Goal → Criteria → Alternatives](/learnings/online/Supply-Chain-Digitization/sc_w2s5_image1.png)

### Saaty Scale (1–9)

<Aside type="note">
| Scale Value | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **1** | Equally important |
| **3** | Moderately more important |
| **5** | Strongly more important |
| **7** | Very strongly more important |
| **9** | Extremely more important |
| **2, 4, 6, 8** | Intermediate values between adjacent judgements |
</Aside>

### AHP Steps

<Steps>
1. **Define the problem** — state the objective clearly
2. **Develop the hierarchical framework** — list all criteria and map their relationships
3. **Construct the Pairwise Comparison Matrix** for each level using the Saaty scale (1–9)
4. **Normalise the matrix** — calculate criterion weights (the priority vector)
5. **Calculate the Consistency Ratio (CR)** using: `CR = CI / RI` where CI = Consistency Index and RI = Random Index (standard table value based on matrix size)
6. **Check CR:** If CR < 0.1 → consistent, proceed. If CR ≥ 0.1 → inconsistent, return to Step 3 and redo the pairwise comparison
</Steps>

<Aside type="note">
**★ CR < 0.1 is the MUST-PASS condition in AHP.** If this threshold is not met, the expert's pairwise judgements are considered internally inconsistent and unreliable — the comparison must be redone.
</Aside>

## Case Study — AHP-Based Inventory Segmentation: XYZ E-tailer

<Badge text="Case Study" variant="tip" />

### Case Setup

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Company** | XYZ E-tail — an e-commerce retailer |
| **Constraint** | Fixed warehouse space under a 3-year contract |
| **Listing policy** | A product is listed as 'available' only if it is physically present in the warehouse |
| **Challenge** | Management wants to expand the product portfolio but warehouse space is limited |
| **Previous policy** | 95% service level maintained uniformly for all SKUs |
| **New plan** | Classify 25 SKUs into 3 groups with differentiated service levels |
| **Tool** | AHP — to classify 25 SKUs into Class A / B / C |

**Target service levels under the new classification:**

| Class | Service Level |
|---|---|
| **A** | 95% |
| **B** | 90% |
| **C** | 85% |

The six criteria used, with their direction of preference:

| # | Criterion | Unit | Direction |
|---|---|---|---|

### Normalisation of SKU Data

**Problem:** All six criteria are in different units — they cannot be directly compared or multiplied.

**Solution:** Normalise all criteria to a 0–1 scale before scoring.

<Aside type="note">
**For Higher-is-Better criteria** (Monthly Demand, Category Priority, Supplier Reliability, Profit Margin):

`Normalised Score = (xᵢ − min) / (max − min)`

**For Lower-is-Better criteria** (Lead Time, Likelihood of Return):

`Normalised Score = 1 − [(xᵢ − min) / (max − min)]`

This inversion converts "lower is better" into a consistent "higher is better" scale — ensuring all six criteria point in the same direction before being multiplied by their AHP weights.
</Aside>

![Normalised SKU Data Table — All 25 SKUs with Normalised Scores Across All 6 Criteria](/learnings/online/Supply-Chain-Digitization/sc_w2s5_image7.png)

---

### Calculating the SKU Score

<Aside type="note">
For each SKU, multiply each normalised criterion score by its AHP criterion weight, then sum across all six criteria:

`SKU Score = Σ (AHP Weightᵢ × Normalised Scoreᵢ)`

A higher combined score indicates a more strategically important SKU overall.
</Aside>

---

### ABC Classification from SKU Scores

<Steps>
1. **Sort** all 25 SKUs by combined SKU Score in descending order (highest → most important)
2. **Calculate cumulative score** and the **cumulative % of total score** for each SKU
3. **Apply cut-offs** to assign classes:
   - **Class A** → top SKUs up to ~60% of cumulative score → **7 out of 25 items** → 95% service level
   - **Class B** → next ~25% of cumulative score → **8 out of 25 items** → 90% service level
   - **Class C** → remaining ~15% → **10 out of 25 items** → 85% service level
</Steps>

<Aside type="note">
**★ The cut-off percentages (60% / 25% / 15%) are not universal fixed values.** They are set by the company based on its own business strategy, warehouse capacity constraints, and service level targets. A different company with different constraints would set different cut-offs.
</Aside>
