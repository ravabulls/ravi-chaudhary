## Strategy Hierarchy — Corporate → Functional → Operations

### Corporate / Business Strategy

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** The overarching strategy of a firm — sets the direction for everything else.

It determines:
- What **products and services** the firm will offer
- What **markets** it will operate in (local vs. global)
- What **type of consumer** it targets (price-conscious vs. niche)
- Whether it is **product-oriented or service-oriented**

A firm can be tightly focused (single product line) or highly diversified across sectors — e.g., FMCG + Automobile + Steel + Domestic + International operations simultaneously.
</Aside>

### Operations / SC Strategy

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** The execution layer of corporate strategy — the answer to *how* the corporate strategy will be achieved on the ground.

**Covers:** Manufacturing, supply, distribution, after-sales service, and resource planning.

**Key characteristic:** It is **not a one-time exercise.** Operations strategy is revisited year on year — sometimes twice a year — because markets are dynamic. Customer profiles, regulations, and the external environment keep changing, and the strategy must adapt to keep the firm on course.
</Aside>

<Aside type="tip">
**★ Core Distinction:**
- **Corporate Strategy = WHAT** to do
- **Operations Strategy = HOW** to do it

Both are continuously evolving — not fixed documents.
</Aside>

## Functional Strategies — All Aligned to Corporate Strategy

Every function within a firm requires its own strategy. All functional strategies must align with the overarching corporate strategy:

| Functional Area | Strategy Type |
|---|---|
| Marketing | Marketing Strategy |
| Finance | Finance Strategy |
| Production | Manufacturing Strategy |
| Human Resources | HR Strategy |
| Communications | Advertising Strategy |
| Supply Chain | Operations / SC Strategy |

<Aside type="note">
**Critical insight:** All functional strategies are **intertwined**. A decision made in one functional area ripples through and affects all others. No functional strategy can be designed in isolation.
</Aside>

## Case Example — Standardised FMCG Product for Price-Conscious Consumers

<Badge text="Case Study" variant="tip" />

### Corporate Strategy Setup

| Parameter | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Product** | Standardised FMCG item (e.g., shampoo) |
| **Target market** | Price-conscious consumers |
| **FMCG definition** | Fast Moving Consumer Goods — purchased regularly, available through retail and online channels |
| **Competitive context** | Product is not unique — many similar competing products exist in the market |

**Strategic implication:** Operating in a competitive commodity market means the firm must ensure **positive profit margins** at all times.

**Strategy to maintain margins:** Lower costs + Increase volume — these two objectives cascade into every functional strategy below.

<Aside type="note">
**★ Key strategic chain:**

Corporate Strategy (price-conscious, standard FMCG) → **Lower cost + Higher volume** → Decisions cascade into Design, Marketing, and Operations.
</Aside>

---

### 1. Product Design & R&D Strategy

<Badge text="Functional Strategy 1" variant="note" />

**Goal:** Lower the unit manufacturing cost without compromising product functionality.

#### Material Choice

| Option | Consideration |
|---|---|
| **Recycled materials** | Often cheaper if the recycling technology cost is low — preferred for cost-conscious strategy |
| **Virgin materials** | May be cheaper if recycling technology is expensive — a context-dependent decision |

**Constraint:** The material choice must not reduce product functionality or drive consumers away from the product.

#### Standardised Components

<Aside type="note">
Avoid unique or highly customised materials — for example, rare dye colours in a shampoo formulation.

**Why:** Unique materials → difficult to procure → limited supplier base → higher purchase cost.

**Instead:** Use **commonly available, standardised materials** → easier to procure → multiple supplier options → lower cost.

*Example: Common shampoo colours are yellow, pink, and white — not fluorescent or proprietary hues.*
</Aside>

#### Modularity

Design the product using **modular components** that are standard and readily available in the market. Standardised parts are assembled into a unique consumer offering — achieving differentiation without a customised cost structure.

<Aside type="tip">
**Application of Concepts:** Modularity is widely used in electronics and flat-pack furniture — components are pre-manufactured and available at scale; assembly is the only variable step. Applied to FMCG, this means standard sub-formulas or base ingredients combined in a unique final product.
</Aside>

#### Packaging Decisions

Packaging must be designed to **target the price-conscious consumer directly**:

| Pack Format | Target Consumer | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| **Sachets** | Low-income, rural, or individual consumers | Small affordable units with low per-unit cost |
| **Bottles** | Family or regular users | Larger units with better per-ml value |

<Aside type="tip">
**Application of Concepts — India context:** 1-rupee shampoo sachets are a defining example of price-accessible FMCG. Many consumers purchase multiple sachets per week instead of a bottle — the sachet format unlocks a segment that would otherwise be priced out entirely. The critical SC decision is the **proportion to package as sachets vs. bottles** based on the geographic and income mix of the target market.
</Aside>

---

### 2. Advertising & Marketing Strategy

<Badge text="Functional Strategy 2" variant="note" />

**Goal:** Price the product to appeal to price-conscious consumers and drive high purchase volume.

#### Stable Pricing

<Aside type="note">
Do not vary price too frequently — pricing consistency builds consumer trust and purchase predictability.

**Retail tool:** **EDLP (Everyday Low Pricing)** — the same stable price daily, with small regular discounts passed through to the consumer. The consumer perceives consistent cost savings in the channel, which increases purchase preference and loyalty.
</Aside>

#### Volume-Driving Tactics

| Tactic | Mechanism |
|---|---|
| **Product bundling** | Sell multiple units together at a package discount — e.g., buy 10 units and receive a discount → drives higher volume per purchase |
| **Strategic discounting** | Applied at specific points to push higher unit volumes without eroding the everyday price anchor |

---

### 3. Operations Strategy

<Badge text="Functional Strategy 3" variant="caution" />

**Goal:** Reduce the cost of operations after product design and pricing decisions are already fixed.

Once design and pricing parameters are set, the operations function must optimise *how* those decisions are executed as efficiently as possible.

#### Key Levers

| Lever | Action |
|---|---|
| **Process efficiency** | Identify and remove duplicate or redundant processes; maximise resource utilisation across all production stages |
| **Logistics optimisation** | Achieve **Full Truck Loads (FTL)** in transportation — avoid partially filled vehicles which waste fixed transport cost |
| **Procurement** | Negotiate aggressively with vendors for lower purchase costs on raw and packaging materials |
| **Inventory** | Reduce inventory levels through **lean practices** — hold only what is needed, when it is needed |
| **Asset management** | High asset ownership (manufacturing equipment, vehicles) → high fixed costs. **Outsourcing** selected activities reduces asset ownership and converts fixed costs to variable costs |

## Key Insight — All Strategies Are Intertwined

<Aside type="note">
Supply chain design and operations strategy **cannot be designed in isolation.** Every functional strategy is connected:

- **Product Design** — determines what materials, components, and packaging formats are needed → shapes procurement strategy
- **Pricing Strategy** — determines target volume and margin requirements → shapes production scale decisions
- **Operations** — determines how to manufacture, distribute, and deliver efficiently → must align with both design and pricing constraints

A change in any one functional strategy **ripples through all others**. Operations strategy must always be designed in **alignment with corporate strategy** — not as a standalone exercise.
</Aside>

## Summary — Corporate Strategy → Functional Strategy Cascade

<Steps>
1. **Corporate Strategy** → Standard FMCG product for a price-conscious market → primary objectives: **Lower cost + Higher volume**
2. **Design & R&D Strategy** → Recycled or standardised materials; modular component design; optimal sachet-to-bottle packaging mix
3. **Marketing Strategy** → Stable EDLP pricing; product bundling; strategic discounting to drive volume
4. **Operations Strategy** → Remove process redundancy; achieve FTL in logistics; lean inventory; outsourcing; aggressive vendor negotiation
</Steps>

<Aside type="tip">
**★ Exam Tip:** If asked about operations strategy in any exam question, always connect it **upward** to corporate strategy (the *what*) and **sideways** to all other functional strategies (Design, Marketing, Finance, HR). Never treat operations strategy as a standalone functional decision — it is always a derivative of the corporate direction and a peer of all other functional strategies.

</Aside>
