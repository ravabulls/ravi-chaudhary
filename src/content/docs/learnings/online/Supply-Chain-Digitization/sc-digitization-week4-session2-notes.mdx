## Quick Recap — Session 1

<Aside type="note">
Supply chains are complex because of:
- Many players and many transactions across multiple paths
- Poor visibility of information across the chain
- The need for independent decision-making at each node

Every player — buyer, seller, or intermediary — must continuously **search, evaluate, and decide**. This session shows what happens when those three activities are poorly supported by information.
</Aside>

---

## Why Platform Economy? — The Problem Statement

<Aside type="note">
**Core Definition of SC Inefficiency:** Inefficiency in a supply chain occurs when the right information does not reach the right player at the right time — causing missed transactions and lost value.
</Aside>

### Example 1 — The Spice Search (B2C, Information Gap)

<Badge text="B2C Example" variant="note" />

**Setup:** A customer in Rajasthan wants to cook a specific dish that requires a rare spice. The spice is not available locally.

**Customer's options without information:**

| Option | Outcome |
|---|---|
| Cook without the spice | Need goes unmet — unsatisfying outcome |
| Substitute with another ingredient | Need is partially met — compromise decision |

**The missed opportunity:**

The spice *is* available — in Delhi. But the customer does not know this. Had the customer known, they could have called the vendor, placed a delivery order, or arranged to collect it.

<Aside type="note">
**Root cause:** Information about product availability is not visible to the buyer.

Value is not created → a transaction that could have happened does not → inefficiency in the value chain.
</Aside>

---

### Example 2 — The Compressor Manufacturer (B2B, Reach & Transaction Cost)

<Badge text="B2B Example" variant="caution" />

**Setup:** Company XYZ manufactures high-quality compressors and wants to sell internationally. Their current approach: send salespersons to international exhibitions.

**The sales process:**

<Steps>
1. Demo the product at the exhibition → network with potential buyers
2. Convince a mid-size manufacturer to consider the product
3. Multiple rounds of negotiation, site visits, and competitive bidding
4. Result: **1 sale made** after a long, expensive process
</Steps>

**The problem:**

| Issue | Detail |
|---|---|
| **Low volume** | One sale cannot recover the cost of exhibition attendance, travel, and salesperson time |
| **High customer acquisition cost** | More exhibitions + more salespeople needed to scale → cost grows proportionally with every new customer |
| **No leverage** | With only a few buyers, XYZ has limited negotiating power on pricing |

**The missed opportunity:**

If XYZ could reach more buyers faster — at lower cost — volume would grow, negotiating power would improve, and the business model would become viable.

<Aside type="note">
**Root cause:** Inability to scale outreach efficiently. The traditional route (exhibitions + salespeople) is high-cost and low-throughput — it cannot match the potential demand that exists globally.
</Aside>

---

### Example 3 — The Food Delivery Matching Problem (B2C, Matching & Decision)

<Badge text="B2C Matching Example" variant="danger" />

**Setup:** A company offers same-day or 10-minute delivery from cloud kitchens. A customer orders two items from two nearby cloud kitchens — for example, a main dish and a dessert.

**What happens (current system):** Two separate delivery partners are dispatched — one per kitchen — resulting in two separate doorbell rings.

**Problems created — even though the need is technically met:**

| Consequence | Impact |
|---|---|
| Poor customer experience | Two separate deliveries feel fragmented and unprofessional |
| Risk of losing the customer | Customer may stop ordering from one or both kitchens in future |
| Competitor switch risk | Frustrated customer may switch to a competitor platform entirely |
| Second customer unserved | Both delivery partners are occupied → another customer who places an order gets no delivery → a second lost transaction |

**The missed opportunity:**

One delivery partner could have picked up both items — the two kitchens are nearby. The second partner would then be free to serve another customer. Two customers served; two orders fulfilled; better resource utilisation.

<Aside type="note">
**Root cause:** Lack of information visibility + poor matching and assignment decision. The system did not have a real-time view of delivery partner locations, kitchen proximity, or pending orders — and therefore could not make the optimal dispatch decision.
</Aside>

## Common Thread Across All 3 Examples

| Example | Problem Type | Core Issue |
|---|---|---|
| **Spice (B2C)** | Information gap | Buyer unaware that supplier and product exist |
| **Compressor (B2B)** | Reach & transaction cost | Seller cannot scale outreach cost-efficiently |
| **Food delivery (B2C)** | Matching / assignment | Wrong resource assigned to wrong order |

All three problems reduce to **two root causes**:

<Aside type="note">
**Root Cause 1 — Visibility of information:** Who has it? Who needs it? The gap between those two questions is where value is lost.

**Root Cause 2 — Decision-making quality:** The right resource must reach the right customer or supplier at the right time. Poor information → poor decisions → missed transactions.

These two gaps → missed opportunities → value not created → SC inefficiency → **need for a platform.**
</Aside>

## Platform Economy — Concept & Definition

### What is a Platform?

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** An intermediary — physical or virtual — that makes business information visible to multiple players and connects them to enable transactions.

A platform is not just a marketplace. It is an **information infrastructure** that reduces search cost, improves decision quality, and enables transactions between parties who would otherwise not find each other.
</Aside>

**What a platform provides:**

| Visibility Element | Examples |
|---|---|
| **Players** | Buyers, sellers, logistics providers, financial institutions, government bodies |
| **Prices** | Current market prices across multiple sellers in one view |
| **Product / service characteristics** | Images, specifications, ratings, reviews |
| **Locations** | Seller locations, delivery zones, pickup points |

**What a platform enables:**

| Capability | Description |
|---|---|
| **Faster decision-making** | All relevant information in one place → less time spent searching |
| **Reduced bias** | Objective information replaces subjective seller claims |
| **Transactional trust** | Players feel safe transacting with unknown parties through the platform's security layer |
| **Hybrid transactions** | Both social and economic interactions — not just buying and selling |

---

### Multisided Platform

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** A platform that caters to several types of players — called *sides* — simultaneously.

Example sides on an e-commerce platform: buyers, sellers, logistics providers, financial institutions, technology providers — all affiliated with and operating through one platform.

**Affiliation requirement:** All parties must be registered or affiliated with the platform. This provides identity verification and a basic security layer for every participant — enabling trust between strangers.
</Aside>

## Examples of Platform Economy

### Traditional (Offline) Platforms

<Badge text="Offline" variant="note" />

| Platform | Why It Qualifies as a Platform |
|---|---|
| **Local markets / local shops** | Multiple buyers and sellers meet at one physical location; both social and economic transactions occur |
| **Matchmaking broker (marriage broker)** | Intermediary reviews profiles of both parties, evaluates compatibility parameters, and suggests matches — classic two-sided platform logic |

<Aside type="note">
Traditional platforms = primarily offline, in-person interactions. The platform economy concept is not new — digital platforms have simply made it vastly more scalable, faster, and global.
</Aside>

---

### Digital Platforms

<Badge text="Digital" variant="caution" />

| Platform | Type | What It Does |
|---|---|---|
| **Amazon, Flipkart** | B2C e-commerce | Buyers, sellers, logistics providers, and payment institutions all on one platform |
| **Ola, Uber** | Aggregator | Matches drivers with customers / commuters in real time |
| **Facebook** | Social + marketplace | Primarily social; marketplace functionality enables economic transactions |
| **Shaadi.com** | Digital matchmaking | Traditional matchmaking digitised — same two-sided platform logic as the marriage broker, now at scale |
| **Ariba (SAP)** | B2B procurement | Companies connect with suppliers for sourcing and procurement; widely used in large organisations |
| **GEM (Government e-Marketplace, India)** | B2G / B2B | Government and private entities purchase products through a common platform; includes decision support tools |

---

## Key Features of the Digital Platform Economy

- **Virtual infrastructure** → enables both online and offline (hybrid) transactions
  - *Example: Pay online via UPI, or pay the delivery partner in cash on delivery*
- **Decision support tools** → e.g., "Similar products you may like" recommendations on Amazon — reducing the buyer's search effort and improving selection quality
- **Registered membership** → all users must be affiliated or logged in → platform provides a baseline security and trust layer for all participants
- **Monetisation of seller attention** → sellers can pay the platform to promote or advertise products — creating a revenue model for the platform itself
- **Multiple parties, one platform** → customers, sellers, logistics partners, financial institutions, and tech providers all operate within the same ecosystem

## Players in a Digital Platform — E-Commerce Model

![Digital Platform Ecosystem Diagram — Hub-and-spoke showing Customer, Seller, Logistics, Financial Institution, and Tech Provider all connected to the central Platform node](/learnings/online/Supply-Chain-Digitization/sc_w4s2_image1.png)

**How the ecosystem operates in practice:**

<Steps>
1. **Customer logs in** → browses products → makes a purchase decision using platform-provided information (ratings, reviews, prices, images)
2. **Seller** → lists and promotes products on the platform; manages inventory and fulfilment within the platform's framework
3. **Platform matches seller to buyer** → one or more logistics partners are assigned to handle physical delivery
4. **Financial institution** → enables digital payment in a manner transparent and auditable to all relevant parties
5. **All players require affiliation / login** → the platform provides the security and trust layer that makes strangers comfortable transacting with each other
</Steps>

## Session Summary — Platform Economy at a Glance

<Aside type="note">
**★ Key Takeaway:** A platform economy connects multiple players — buyers, sellers, service providers, and institutions — through a physical or virtual intermediary. It makes information visible and enables faster, safer, and more scalable transactions.

**Solves:** Information asymmetry | Poor matching and assignment | High transaction and customer acquisition costs

**Enables:** Social and economic transactions | Online and offline (hybrid) operations

**Key concept to know:** Multisided platform — one platform, multiple types of participants, all affiliated and interacting through a common infrastructure.
</Aside>
