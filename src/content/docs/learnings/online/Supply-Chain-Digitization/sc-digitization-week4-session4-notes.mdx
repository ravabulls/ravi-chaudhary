## Session Agenda & Context

<Aside type="note">
**Why this example?** SC coordination asks: how should decisions be made within a supply chain — centrally or independently? The News Vendor Problem (NVP) is the classic model that reveals what goes wrong when each player optimises for themselves alone.

**Focus decision:** "How much to order?" — a lot-sizing and order quantity decision.

**Relationship to EOQ:** Both deal with order quantity, but they are different models. EOQ = multi-period, continuous demand. NVP = **single-period**, one-shot ordering decision.

**Link to platform economy:** This session builds the foundation — SC coordination failures → platform economy as the structural solution (covered in later sessions).
</Aside>

<Aside type="tip">
**★ Exam Tip:** News Vendor Problem = **single-period ordering decision**. This definition distinguishes it from EOQ. Always state this when introducing the model.
</Aside>

## The News Vendor Setting — Players & Objectives

### Players

| Player | Role | Objective |
|---|---|---|
| **News Vendor (NV)** | Observes market demand → places order → sells to end market | Maximise **own** profit |
| **Regional Supplier (RS)** | Receives NV's order → decides whether to print and supply | Maximise **own** profit — can choose **not to supply** if the order is not viable |

<Aside type="note">
In practice, multiple News Vendors and multiple Regional Suppliers can exist simultaneously. This analysis simplifies to **one NV and one RS** to isolate the coordination and incentive problem cleanly.

**Other possible objectives** for SC players include cost minimisation and emission targets — but this model uses profit maximisation throughout.
</Aside>

### Decision Sequence (Chronological)

<Steps>
1. **NV observes demand** → decides order quantity **S** → places order to RS
2. **RS receives order** → decides whether to accept and supply (based on RS's own profitability assessment)
</Steps>

<Aside type="tip">
**★ Exam Tip — The critical distinction:**
- **Decentralised** = each player maximises their own profit independently — NV and RS each decide alone
- **Centralised** = a central planner maximises **total SC profit** — both players' outcomes are optimised together

This distinction drives every scenario analysis that follows.
</Aside>

## Revenue & Cost Parameters

| Parameter | News Vendor (₹/unit) | Regional Supplier (₹/unit) |
|---|---|---|
| **Retail price (p)** | ₹30 (price charged to end customer) | — |
| **Wholesale price (w)** | ₹10 (paid to RS per unit ordered) | ₹10 (received from NV per unit supplied) |
| **Distribution cost (c_d)** | ₹10 per unit sold | — |
| **Manufacturing / operating cost (c_m)** | — | ₹5 per unit printed |
| **Fixed facility cost (F)** | No fixed cost → always viable if demand > 0 | ₹1,50,000 per period |
| **Net margin per unit sold** | ₹30 − ₹10 − ₹10 = **₹10 per unit** | ₹10 − ₹5 = **₹5 per unit** (above breakeven) |
| **Breakeven quantity** | No fixed cost — always profitable if any unit sells | ₹1,50,000 ÷ ₹5 = **30,000 units** |

## Key Formulas

### News Vendor Profit

<Aside type="note">
**Units sold = min(Demand D, Order S)**
→ The NV can only sell what demand allows or what was ordered — whichever is lower.

**NV Profit = [min(D, S) × (p − c_d)] − [S × w]**
= [min(D, S) × ₹20] − [S × ₹10]

| Condition | Outcome |
|---|---|
| **D = S** | NV sells everything → Profit = S × ₹10 |
| **D < S** | NV sells only D units → overstocking loss on (S − D) units procured but unsold |
| **D > S** | NV sells only S units → lost sales (underage) |
</Aside>

### Regional Supplier Profit

<Aside type="note">
**RS Profit = [S × (w − c_m)] − F**
= [S × ₹5] − ₹1,50,000

| Condition | Outcome |
|---|---|
| **S < 30,000** | RS makes a loss → has a rational motive to **refuse the order** |
| **S = 30,000** | RS breaks exactly even → barely viable |
| **S > 30,000** | Each additional unit earns ₹5 contribution margin → profitable |
</Aside>

## Deterministic Setting — What It Means

<Aside type="note">
**Deterministic demand:** Demand is known with certainty before the order is placed. It can be a fixed constant or a function of price (as price increases, demand decreases).

**Why use it?** It is the analytical baseline — it isolates the **incentive misalignment problem** from the demand uncertainty problem. Removing demand uncertainty lets us see the coordination failure in its purest form.

**Is it realistic?** No — but that is not its purpose. The probabilistic setting (next session) adds demand uncertainty back in.
</Aside>

<Aside type="tip">
**★ Exam Tip:** Deterministic ≠ realistic. Always explain *why* a setting is chosen — deterministic is used to isolate and analyse the incentive problem without adding the complexity of demand uncertainty simultaneously.
</Aside>

## 7 Scenarios — Decentralised Decision Making (Deterministic)

NV places order S given known demand D. Both players make independent profit-maximising decisions.

| Scenario | Demand (D) | Order (S) | NV Profit (₹) | RS Profit (₹) | Who Profits? | Would NV Choose This? |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | 0 | 2,000 | −₹20,000 | −₹1,40,000 | Neither | No — better to order 0 |
| **2** | 2,000 | 40,000 | −₹3,60,000 | +₹50,000 | Supplier only | No — NV loses badly |
| **3** | 2,000 | 1,500 | +₹15,000 | −₹1,42,500 | NV only | No — S4 is better |
| **4** | 2,000 | 2,000 | +₹20,000 | −₹1,40,000 | NV only | **Yes** (D < 30K; best NV option) |
| **5** | 30,000 | 30,000 | +₹3,00,000 | ₹0 (breakeven) | NV only | **Yes** (NV profitable) |
| **6** | 40,000 | 40,000 | +₹4,00,000 | +₹50,000 | **Both ✓** | **Yes — both win** |
| **7** | > 40,000 | = D | Increases with D | Increases with D | **Both ✓** | **Yes — best outcome** |

---

## Scenario-by-Scenario Analysis

### Scenarios 1–3: Demand Very Low (D ≪ Breakeven)

<Badge text="D = 0 or 2,000 | RS Breakeven = 30,000" variant="danger" />

**Scenario 1 (D=0, S=2,000):** NV sells 0 units → loses ₹20,000. RS prints 2,000 → loses ₹1,40,000. Neither player profits. NV would never choose this — ordering 0 is better.

**Scenario 2 (D=2,000, S=40,000):** NV massively overstocks → loses ₹3,60,000. RS gets a large order → profits ₹50,000. Supplier profits but NV is destroyed. NV would never rationally choose this.

**Scenario 3 (D=2,000, S=1,500):** NV slightly understocks → profits ₹15,000. RS order is far below breakeven → loses ₹1,42,500.

<Aside type="note">
**Critical insight:** Scenarios 1–3 will not actually occur in a decentralised setting. The NV always has a better option available — Scenario 4. These scenarios are included only to show why the NV always converges to ordering S = D.
</Aside>

---

### Scenario 4: D = 2,000, S = 2,000 (NV Matches Demand)

<Badge text="NV Profits — RS Loses — SC Conflict" variant="danger" />

- **NV sells all 2,000 units → Profit = 2,000 × ₹10 = +₹20,000 ✓**
- **RS prints 2,000 (well below 30,000 breakeven) → Loss = −₹1,40,000 ✗**

<Aside type="note">
**★ Key conflict point:** NV is profitable and has rationally made the best decision available to it. RS is deeply unprofitable.

**RS's rational response:** Refuse to supply the order. If RS refuses → NV has no product to sell → SC collapses. The NV's individually rational decision inadvertently destroys the supply chain.
</Aside>

---

### Scenario 5: D = 30,000, S = 30,000 (Demand = RS Breakeven)

<Badge text="NV Profits — RS Breaks Even" variant="caution" />

- **NV Profit = 30,000 × ₹10 = +₹3,00,000 ✓**
- **RS Profit = 30,000 × ₹5 − ₹1,50,000 = ₹0 (exactly breakeven)**

NV is profitable. RS is indifferent — barely viable, no incentive to invest or expand capacity. The SC technically functions, but only just.

---

### Scenarios 6 & 7: D ≥ 40,000 (Both Players Profitable)

<Badge text="Both Profit — Aligned Incentives" variant="tip" />

**Scenario 6 (D = 40,000, S = 40,000):**
- NV Profit = +₹4,00,000 ✓
- RS Profit = (40,000 × ₹5) − ₹1,50,000 = +₹50,000 ✓

**Scenario 7 (D > 40,000, S = D):**
- Both profits increase proportionally with demand
- Both players prefer S7 > S6 > S5 — incentives are fully aligned when D ≥ 30,000

<Aside type="tip">
**Application of Concepts:** Note that NV and RS have aligned incentives **only when demand is high enough to cover the supplier's fixed costs**. This is the fundamental coordination problem — the NV's order quantity decision has externalities for the RS, but the NV has no incentive to internalise those externalities when deciding how much to order.
</Aside>

## Key Insights — Decentralised vs. Centralised

### News Vendor's Decision Rule (Decentralised)

<Aside type="note">
In a decentralised, deterministic setting, the NV's dominant strategy is always:

**Order exactly S = D** (match demand precisely)

| Rule | Reason |
|---|---|
| Never order S > D | Overstocking hurts the NV — unsold units generate a procurement cost but no revenue |
| Never order S < D | Understocking leaves money on the table — profitable sales foregone |
| Always order S = D | Maximises NV's own profit in every demand scenario |

**The NV holds the power:** The NV's order quantity determines what the RS earns. The supplier's commercial fate is entirely in the NV's hands.
</Aside>

---

### Impact of NV's Decision on the Regional Supplier

| Demand Level | NV's Rational Order | NV Outcome | RS Outcome | SC Outcome |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **D < 30,000** | S = D (< 30,000) | Profitable | Loss — may refuse to supply | **SC conflict → potential collapse** |
| **D < 30,000, S forced > 30,000** | NV would never choose this | Loss | Profitable | NV refuses — not chosen |
| **D ≥ 30,000** | S = D (≥ 30,000) | Profitable | Profitable or breakeven | **SC works ✓** |

<Aside type="note">
**★ The core finding of decentralised SC analysis:**

The NV maximises its own profit → inadvertently causes SC conflict when market demand is below the supplier's breakeven. The SC is not optimised as a whole — it is optimised only from the NV's perspective.

This is called **double marginalisation** in SC literature — each player takes a margin, and the cumulative effect reduces system-wide efficiency below what a central planner could achieve.
</Aside>

---

### What Would Change Under Centralised Decision Making?

A central planner optimising **total SC profit** would:

- Only operate the SC when D ≥ 30,000 — ensuring both players are viable
- Choose order quantities that guarantee RS profitability alongside NV profitability
- Recognise that an order of S < 30,000 makes the system unviable — and either refuse to operate or find a way to compensate RS

<Aside type="note">
**The key challenge:** How do you get the NV to voluntarily order more than what demand dictates — even when doing so hurts the NV's own margin?

This requires **coordination contracts** — mechanisms that change the incentive structure so that the NV's individually rational decision also becomes the collectively optimal decision.

**Examples of coordination contracts** (covered in upcoming sessions): Buyback contracts | Revenue sharing | Quantity discounts
</Aside>

## Limitation of the Deterministic Setting

<Aside type="note">
The deterministic setting assumed demand was known with certainty before the order was placed. This is analytically clean but unrealistic in practice.

**Real demand is probabilistic:** The NV does not know exactly how many papers will sell tomorrow. Demand is a random variable — it could be high or low.

The conflict scenarios (1–4) above become **structurally more complex** when demand is uncertain — because the NV must now decide S before knowing D, adding a new source of misalignment.

**Next session:** The same NVP in a probabilistic demand setting → additional analytical challenges emerge → sets up the full coordination mechanisms and platform economy discussion.
</Aside>
