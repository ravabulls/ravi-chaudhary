## SC Stakeholders & Their Roles

<Aside type="note">
Not all stakeholders are present in every supply chain — the players involved depend entirely on the SC type.
</Aside>

| Stakeholder | Role |
|---|---|
| **Suppliers** | Provide / sell raw materials or components needed for the final product |
| **Manufacturers** | Convert raw materials into finished goods through various production processes |
| **Transporters (Logistics)** | Move goods from RM supplier → manufacturer, and from finished goods → rest of the distribution network |
| **Distributors** | Store RM and finished goods; release to the next stage based on production schedules or demand |
| **Retailers** | Closest to the customer; sell finished goods via online or physical stores |
| **Consumers** | End users — the point at which the supply chain terminates |

## Forward SC vs. Reverse SC

### Forward Supply Chain

<Steps>
1. **Supplier** — provides raw materials
2. **Manufacturer** — converts RM into finished goods
3. **Distributor** — stores and moves finished goods
4. **Retailer** — sells to the end customer
5. **Customer** — receives the product
</Steps>

The **transporter** connects every link in this chain. The retailer is the last commercial player before the customer.

### Reverse Supply Chain

When a customer returns a product, it flows **backward** through the chain:

| Return Type | Handled By |
|---|---|
| **Minor repair** | Retailer or distributor — product is repaired and returned to the customer |
| **Major repair / exchange** | Routed back to the manufacturer |

<Aside type="tip">
**Application of Concepts:** Managing returns is now essential for customer satisfaction in today's market — especially in e-commerce, where return rates can exceed 20–30%. Both forward and reverse supply chains must be actively designed and managed. SC must now be viewed with *new lenses*.
</Aside>

## Value Chain vs. Supply Chain

These two concepts are often confused — they involve the same processes but have fundamentally different objectives.

### Value Chain

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** A focus on **adding value** at each step — through planning, design, development, and delivery — with the objective of enhancing the customer experience and justifying premium pricing.

**Output:** Competitive advantage — helps a company perform better than its rivals.
</Aside>

<Badge text="Example: Coffee Shop" variant="tip" />

<Steps>
1. Source high-quality coffee beans from a renowned growing region
2. In-house roasting to preserve quality and flavour
3. Welcoming store ambience with comfortable seating
4. Personalised service and customisation options for every customer
</Steps>

*Result:* Every step adds value → the customer willingly pays a premium price.

---

### Supply Chain

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** A focus on the **efficient movement and delivery** of goods from supplier to end customer — ensuring the product reaches the customer in the right manner, at the right time, driving customer satisfaction.
</Aside>

<Badge text="Example: Smartphone" variant="tip" />

<Steps>
1. Source components (microchips, screens, batteries) from global suppliers
2. Transport components to assembly plants across multiple locations
3. Assemble into finished smartphones
4. Distribute to global retailer stores to fulfil customer demand
</Steps>

*Focus:* Timely component availability, efficient production, minimal wastage, and global distribution.

---

### Summary: The Key Distinction

<Aside type="note">
**Value Chain = *WHY* value is added** (building competitive edge)

**Supply Chain = *HOW* goods move efficiently** (operational execution)
</Aside>

## Competitive Strategy

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** Searching for a favourable competitive position within an industry — achieving a profitable and sustainable position against market forces through advantages such as strong brand recognition and economies of scale.
</Aside>

| Strategy Type | Example | Approach |
|---|---|---|
| **Cost Leadership** | Retail chain stores | Large volume, wide product range offered at the lowest cost |
| **Differentiation** | Smartphone brands | Frequent new models with new technology; short launch gaps keep customers engaged |
| **Volume + Low Price** | Hypermarkets | All products under one roof (department store + grocery); high volume → low pricing → high footfall |

<Aside type="tip">
**Key Takeaway:** Just as no two supply chains are identical, no two competitive strategies are identical — the right strategy depends on the competitive position a company wants to achieve in its market.
</Aside>

## Competitive Priorities

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** The critical operational dimensions a process or supply chain must possess to satisfy its customers. These priorities drive decisions on capacity, technology, production process design, and planning & control systems.

A company does **not** need to excel at all five — the most relevant ones must be selected based on competitive strategy.
</Aside>

| Priority | What It Means |
|---|---|
| **Cost** | Minimise cost at every step of the SC or manufacturing process → enables a low-pricing strategy |
| **Quality** | All products and processes comply with specifications; product is accepted on quality terms |
| **Flexibility** | Ability to offer varying volumes *and* varying product mix |
| **Delivery** | Reliable and quick delivery; stock availability when demand arises |
| **Innovativeness** | Speed of new product introduction → low time-to-market; fast implementation of new processes |

## Manufacturing Processes — 5 Types

<Badge text="Core Concept" variant="note" />

Competitive priorities determine which manufacturing process is most appropriate. There are five types, ordered from highest variety / lowest volume to lowest variety / highest volume.

### The 5 Process Types

| # | Type | Characteristics | Volume | Variety | Flow | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Project** | Product stays stationary; all processes brought to it | Very Low | Very High | Stationary | Construction, ships, aircraft |
| 2 | **Job Shop** | Work centres group similar tasks; product moves through relevant centres | Low–Medium | High | Jumbled | Custom furniture workshop |
| 3 | **Batch Production** | Goods made in batches; each batch goes through the same series of steps | Medium | Medium | Disconnected line | Bakery production |
| 4 | **Assembly Line** | High volume, standardised items; products move through stations in sequence | High | Low | Sequential | Automobile manufacturing |
| 5 | **Continuous Process** | Uninterrupted flow; fixed sequence of steps; common for liquids and gases | Very High | Very Low | Continuous | Petroleum refining |

<Aside type="note">
**Rule of thumb:** Moving from Project → Continuous = increasing standardisation, increasing volume, and decreasing variety.
</Aside>

## Linking It All Together

This session's concepts form a single connected strategic chain:

<Steps>
1. **Competitive Strategy** — defines the market position the company wants (cost leadership, differentiation, volume)
2. **Competitive Priorities** — translates strategy into operational dimensions (cost, quality, flexibility, delivery, innovativeness)
3. **Manufacturing Process** — selected based on priorities (project → job shop → batch → assembly → continuous)
4. **SC Type** — determined by the manufacturing process chosen
</Steps>

### SC Type Linked to Manufacturing Process

| SC Type | Focus | Linked Manufacturing Process |
|---|---|---|
| **Responsive SC** | Product reaches the customer fast, as per customer requirements | High-variety production — Job Shop or Batch |
| **Efficient SC** | Deliver at minimum cost | High-volume standardised production — Assembly Line or Continuous |

<Aside type="tip">
**Exam Angle:** *"How does competitive strategy translate into SC design?"*

Answer framework: **Strategy → Priorities** (cost / quality / flexibility / delivery / innovativeness) **→ Manufacturing Process → Responsive or Efficient SC.**

Use examples from this session to ground each step of the chain.
</Aside>
