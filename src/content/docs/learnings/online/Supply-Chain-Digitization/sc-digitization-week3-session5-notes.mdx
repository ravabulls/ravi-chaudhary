## Example Context — The Bottling Plant

<Aside type="note">
**Firm:** A soft drink bottling plant reviewing its procurement strategy.

**Scope:** Plant-level analysis — not organisation-level. This distinction matters: the number and types of items will differ significantly depending on which level the matrix is applied at.

**Data available:** Purchase quantity (packs), cost per pack, total purchase value, number of suppliers, and average distance to supplier.

**Data period:** Last financial year. If no procurement history exists — use market price estimates and planned quantities.

**Items:** 8 representative items chosen for illustration. A real bottling plant would procure many more.
</Aside>

<Aside type="tip">
**★ Exam Tip:** Always state whether the Kraljic Matrix is applied at **plant level** or **organisation level** — the scope determines which items are in scope and how risk and cost thresholds are set.
</Aside>

## Key Observations from the Data

### Profit Impact (Purchase Value)

| Item | Observation |
|---|---|
| **Label A** | Highest total value at ₹75 lakh — the pack size (2,500 labels) makes this look expensive per unit, but the volume is what drives total cost |
| **Plastic Bottle & Syrup** | Also high total value — syrup at ₹6,300/barrel is expensive per unit |
| **Office supplies** | Very low total value — Paper and Ink Cartridge B together total only ~₹1,000 |
| **Carton** | 30,000 units purchased but at ₹20 each — moderate total value despite high volume |

### Supply Risk

| Risk Level | Items | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| **High risk — few suppliers** | Syrup (2), Labels A & B (4 each), Ink Cartridge A (1 supplier only) | Limited sourcing options |
| **Low risk — many suppliers** | Plastic Bottle (1,000), Carton, Paper, Ink Cartridge B | Abundant options |
| **High risk — far distance** | Syrup (international), Ink Cartridge A (very far) | Long replenishment lead times |
| **Low risk — close distance** | Plastic Bottle (very close) | Quick replenishment possible |

<Aside type="note">
**★ Why ABC Analysis Alone is NOT Enough:**

ABC analysis classifies items by purchase value — useful for setting internal inventory policy (review frequency, material handling priority). But it says nothing about supply-side risk and therefore cannot define a procurement strategy.

**Kraljic Matrix = ABC-style value analysis + supply risk dimension → richer, actionable procurement strategy.**

ABC → internal inventory policy. Kraljic → external procurement strategy. Both are needed, but for different purposes.
</Aside>

## Building a Composite Supply Risk Metric

### The Problem with Two Sub-Metrics

The two available supply risk sub-metrics operate in **opposite directions** — they cannot be combined without normalisation:

| Sub-metric | Direction | Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **Number of suppliers** | More suppliers → **lower** risk | Inverse relationship |
| **Average distance** | Greater distance → **higher** risk | Direct relationship |

### Scaling Approach

All metrics are converted to comparable scales before combining:

| Metric | Scale | Logic |
|---|---|---|
| **Profit Impact** | 0–10 | 0 = lowest value item, 10 = highest value item |
| **No. of Suppliers** | 1–10 | 1 = fewest suppliers = highest risk; 10 = most suppliers = lowest risk |
| **Avg. Distance** | 1–10 | 1 = farthest = highest risk; 10 = closest = lowest risk |

<Aside type="note">
The professor uses 0–10 for Profit Impact and 1–10 for supply risk sub-metrics — a minor scaling inconsistency that does not materially affect the analysis. In practice, prefer consistent scales throughout (either all 0–10 or all 1–10).
</Aside>

### Weighted Supply Risk Formula

<Aside type="note">
**Weighted Supply Risk = 0.8 × (No. of Suppliers Score) + 0.2 × (Distance Score)**

**Why these weights?**
- Weight **0.8** on number of suppliers → the company judges that supplier availability is the more dominant driver of supply risk
- Weight **0.2** on distance → distance is manageable in most contexts, but should be increased significantly in poor terrain or remote operating environments

**Critical principle:** Weights are **judgement-based** — the company decides based on its specific operating context. There is no universal rule for weighting. Always justify the weights chosen based on context when answering an exam question.
</Aside>

## Plotting & Classification on the Kraljic Matrix

- **X-axis:** Profit Impact (0–10)
- **Y-axis:** Weighted Supply Risk (1–10)
- **Cutoff applied:** Score > 6 on either axis = classified into the "high" side of that dimension

### Resulting Quadrant Classification

| Quadrant | Items |
|---|---|
| **Strategic** (High PI + High Risk) | Syrup, Label A |
| **Bottleneck** (Low PI + High Risk) | Label B, Carton, Ink Cartridge A (labelling) |
| **Leverage** (High PI + Low Risk) | Plastic Bottle |
| **Non-Critical** (Low PI + Low Risk) | Paper (A4), Ink Cartridge B (office) |

<Aside type="note">
**★ Exam Tip — The cutoff caveat:** The cutoff value (here: 6 out of 10) is a **management judgement call** — it is not a universal fixed threshold. Changing the cutoff can reclassify items. Always mention this when explaining the classification in an exam answer. The cutoff should be set based on the firm's risk appetite and strategic priorities.
</Aside>

## Logic Behind Key Recommendations

### Plastic Bottle → Competitive Bidding

With 1,000 suppliers located nearby, the buyer holds **maximum negotiating power**. Remaining with one supplier for five years is a missed cost-reduction opportunity. Opening competitive tendering will drive procurement cost down significantly.

### Syrup → JV / Long-Term Negotiation / Substitutes

Only 2 suppliers globally, with international distance — the textbook definition of a Strategic item challenge. Yearly renegotiation produces cost instability and relationship fragility.

<Aside type="tip">
**Decision tree for Syrup:**
- If the syrup formulation defines the product's identity → substitutes are not feasible → focus on JV or a long-term lock-in deal
- If some flexibility exists on formulation → explore alternative syrups to broaden the supplier base and reduce supply risk
</Aside>

### Label A & B → Long-Term Contracts + Distribute Across Suppliers

Both labels have very few suppliers (4 each) at far locations. Yearly selection creates price instability and fragile relationships.

**Fix:** Multi-year contract with 2–3 suppliers simultaneously — stabilises cost (Label A, Strategic) and reduces disruption risk (Label B, Bottleneck). Distributing procurement across multiple suppliers eliminates single-point-of-failure exposure.

### Ink Cartridge A (Labelling) → Substitute / Nearby Supplier

This is the **most dangerous bottleneck item** in the portfolio — one supplier located very far away. A supply disruption stops in-house label printing, which halts production batching entirely.

**Two options:**
1. Explore supplier takeover if volumes justify it
2. Switch to a standard compatible ink from a local or nearby supplier — a substitute strategy that immediately lowers supply risk

### Non-Critical Items (Paper, Ink Cartridge B) → Paperless / Spot Purchase

Low value and low risk — no strategic oversight required. Continue spot purchasing and explore going paperless or digital to eventually eliminate the procurement need altogether.

<Aside type="note">
**★ Match the intervention to the quadrant.** Do not apply JV logic to Non-Critical items or spot-purchase logic to Strategic items. The quadrant drives the intervention — not cost or quantity alone.
</Aside>

## Continuous Improvement — Keeping the Matrix Relevant

<Aside type="note">
The Kraljic Matrix is **not a one-time exercise**. Markets, supplier landscapes, and costs change continuously. The matrix must be revisited and refined regularly to remain actionable.

**Key ongoing actions:**
- **Review metrics regularly** — revisit metric selection, scaling approach, and weights as context evolves
- **Build team consensus** — involve the full procurement team; others may flag additional risk dimensions not yet captured
- **Benchmark against peers** — look at best practices from other plants or industry peers in the same sector
- **Validate outcomes** — confirm that strategies assigned per segment actually produce the intended results in practice
- **Adjust weights by context** — in poor terrain or remote operating regions, the distance weight should be significantly higher than 0.2
</Aside>

<Aside type="tip">
**★ Exam Tip:** Mentioning continuous improvement and context-specific weighting signals to the examiner that you understand the Kraljic Matrix as a **living management tool**, not a fixed formula applied once and filed away.
</Aside>

## Session Summary — 6-Step Kraljic Application Process

<Steps>
1. **Collect data** — purchase value, number of suppliers, average supplier distance for all items in scope
2. **Scale and weight** — normalise sub-metrics to a consistent scale; compute weighted composite supply risk score
3. **Plot and classify** — apply a management-defined cutoff (e.g. 6/10) to assign each item to one of the four quadrants
4. **Review current state** — compare actual procurement practice against the ideal strategy for each quadrant
5. **Recommend** — define targeted strategy per item: competitive bidding / JV / long-term contract / substitution / spot purchase
6. **Iterate** — revisit the matrix regularly; refine weights, metrics, and cutoffs as market conditions evolve
</Steps>

<Aside type="note">
**★ The Kraljic Matrix turns raw procurement data into a strategic action plan.** The quadrant drives the intervention — not just cost or quantity considered in isolation. Classification is always the first step; strategy follows from it.
</Aside>
