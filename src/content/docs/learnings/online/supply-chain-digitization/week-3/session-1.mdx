## Session Overview

**Topic:** Supply Chain Design & Operations Strategy — linking operations strategy with corporate/functional strategies.

**Why it matters:** Operations strategy determines most firm-level activities — processes, products, employees, and markets. It is not a one-time exercise — it is revisited year on year (sometimes twice a year).

---

## Strategy Hierarchy: Corporate → Functional → Operations

### Corporate / Business Strategy

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** The overarching strategy of a firm — sets the direction for everything else.
</Aside>

**Determines:**
- What products and services the firm will offer
- What markets it will operate in (local vs. global)
- What type of consumer it targets (price-conscious vs. niche)
- Whether it is product-oriented or service-oriented
- Whether the firm is focused (single product) or diversified (FMCG + Auto + Steel + Domestic + International)

### Operations / SC Strategy

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** The execution layer of corporate strategy — how it gets implemented on the ground. Answers: *How will the corporate strategy be achieved?*
</Aside>

**Covers:** Manufacturing, supply, distribution, after-sales service, resource planning.

**Needed because:** Markets are dynamic — customer profiles, regulations, and environment keep changing.

<Aside type="note">
**Corporate Strategy = WHAT to do. Operations Strategy = HOW to do it. Both are continuously evolving.**
</Aside>

### Functional Strategies — All Linked to Corporate

Every function within a firm needs its own strategy — all must align with corporate strategy:

- Marketing Strategy
- Finance Strategy
- Production / Manufacturing Strategy
- HR Strategy
- Advertising Strategy
- Supply Chain / Operations Strategy

<Aside type="caution">
All functional strategies are intertwined — a decision in one affects all others.
</Aside>

## Case Example: Standardized FMCG Product for Price-Conscious Consumers

### Corporate Strategy Setup

- **Product:** Standardized FMCG item (e.g., shampoo)
- **Target market:** Price-conscious consumers
- **FMCG = Fast Moving Consumer Goods** — purchased regularly, available in retail and online
- Product is not unique — many similar competing products in market

### Core Implication

Competitive market → must ensure positive profit margins.

**Strategy to maintain margins:** Lower costs + Increase volume

These two objectives cascade into strategies across all functions.

<Aside type="note">
Key chain: Corporate Strategy (price-conscious, standard FMCG) → Lower cost + Higher volume → Decisions in Design, Marketing, Operations.
</Aside>

---

## 1. Product Design & R&D Strategy

**Goal:** Lower unit manufacturing cost without compromising product functionality.

| Decision Area | Cost-Reduction Approach | Caveat |
|---|---|---|
| **Material choice** | Consider recycled materials over virgin materials | Only if recycling cost is low; virgin materials may be cheaper if recycling tech is expensive |
| **Standardized components** | Avoid unique/customized materials (e.g., rare dye colors) | Unique materials → limited suppliers → higher cost |
| **Modularity** | Design with modular components standard and readily available in market | Assemble standardized parts into a unique consumer offering |
| **Packaging** | Sachets for low-income/rural/individual consumers; bottles for family use | Decide proportion based on market mix |

**Example (India):** ₹1 shampoo sachets are very common; some consumers buy multiple sachets instead of a bottle.

---

## 2. Advertising & Marketing Strategy

**Goal:** Price product to appeal to price-conscious consumers and drive high volume.

| Tactic | Description |
|---|---|
| **Stable pricing** | Do not vary price too much — consistency builds consumer trust |
| **EDLP (Everyday Low Pricing)** | Same price daily; small regular discounts passed to consumer |
| **Product bundling** | Sell multiple units together at a discount — drives higher volume per purchase |
| **Discounting** | Applied strategically to push higher unit volumes |

---

## 3. Operations Strategy

**Goal:** Reduce cost of operations after design and pricing decisions are made.

| Lever | How |
|---|---|
| **Process efficiency** | Remove duplicate/redundant processes; maximize resource utilization |
| **Logistics optimisation** | Achieve Full Truck Loads (FTL) in transportation — avoid partially filled vehicles |
| **Procurement** | Negotiate with vendors for lower purchase costs |
| **Inventory** | Reduce inventory through lean practices |
| **Asset management** | High asset ownership → high fixed cost; consider outsourcing to reduce fixed costs |

---

## Key Insight: All Strategies Are Intertwined

Supply chain design and operations cannot be designed in isolation. A change in one functional strategy ripples through all others.

| Functional Area | Key Questions |
|---|---|
| Product Design | What materials, components, packaging? |
| Pricing Strategy | How much? To whom? |
| Operations | How to manufacture, distribute, and deliver efficiently? |

Operations strategy must always be designed **in alignment with corporate strategy**.

---

## Summary — Corporate Strategy → Functional Strategy Cascade

| Function | Strategy |
|---|---|
| **Corporate** | Standard FMCG for price-conscious market → Lower cost + Higher volume |
| **Design & R&D** | Recycled/standard materials, modular components, sachet + bottle mix |
| **Marketing** | Stable pricing, everyday low price, bundling, discounts |
| **Operations** | Remove redundancy, FTL loads, lean inventory, outsourcing, vendor negotiation |

<Aside type="tip">
**Exam tip:** If asked about operations strategy, always connect it upward to corporate strategy and sideways to other functional strategies. Never treat it as standalone.
</Aside>
