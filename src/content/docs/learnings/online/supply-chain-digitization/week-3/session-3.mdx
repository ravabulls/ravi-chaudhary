## Three Dimensions of Supply Chain Strategy

All three are interdependent — decisions in one affect the others.

### A. Design Strategy

**What:** Structure of the supply chain.

| Decision Area | Options |
|---|---|
| Facilities | No. and location of facilities, warehouses, retail points |
| Retail type | Organised (chain stores) vs. unorganised (kirana, pop-ups, street vendors) |
| Warehousing | Traditional stocking vs. cross-docking/transshipment (8–12 hr hold) |
| Manufacturing | In-house, distributed, or contract manufacturing |
| Channel | Brick & mortar (physical store) vs. e-commerce (distant delivery to customer home) |

### B. Operations Strategy

**What:** Day-to-day activities with long-term SC impact.

- Process organisation inside facility — assembly, storage, labelling, packaging
- Production sequencing — when to make which SKUs, when to dispatch to which location
- Manpower and equipment planning
- Inventory and materials management

### C. Sourcing & Procurement Strategy

**What:** How the SC design is created and sustained through external parties.

- Which vendors, what to buy vs. own, local vs. global sourcing
- Types procured: raw materials, packaging, technology (ERP), equipment
- Own / Purchase / Hire decisions for equipment and facilities

<Aside type="caution">
Design → Operations → Procurement are all linked. Changing one forces adaptation in the others.
</Aside>

## Procurement Strategy — Overview

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** Procurement = process of identifying, contracting, and purchasing from the right supply sources.

Directly impacts financial performance — COGS, asset values, and buyer-supplier relationships.
</Aside>

### Stages of the Procurement Process

<Steps>
1. **Needs Identification** — what, how much, who needs it internally, what budget?
2. **Market Research on Sources** — ongoing activity; new suppliers and technologies enter constantly.
3. **Identification & Evaluation of Suppliers** — shortlist, qualify candidates.
4. **Contracting** — invite quotes/bids/proposals → formalise relationship. Contract can be one-time or long-term depending on product and vendor nature.
5. **Purchasing & Receiving** — physical procurement, payment clearance, systems for receiving.
</Steps>

### Why Procurement Matters

- Affects COGS and asset values directly
- Good buyer-supplier relationships → standardised specs, fewer repeated negotiations
- Streamlines inter-organisational processes
- Procurement and sourcing roles are very competitive and high-value in MNCs

## Make vs. Buy Decision

**The first decision before building any procurement strategy — decide which items/services to make in-house and which to purchase.**

### Reasons to MAKE (in-house)

| Reason | Detail |
|---|---|
| Unique product/service | Competitive differentiator vs. rivals |
| Lower in-house cost | Due to proprietary processes or technologies |
| Easy deployment of internal resources | Staff and equipment already available |
| More control | Over quality, processes, intellectual property |
| Builds long-term capability | Standardised internal procedures |

### Reasons to BUY (procure / outsource)

| Reason | Detail |
|---|---|
| Competitive edge through relationships | Right vendors as strategic partners |
| No need to reinvent the wheel | Vendors already have expertise and market access |
| Vendor capability > in-house capability | Vendor's core competency is your weakness |
| Cost of buying < cost of making | E.g., outsourcing packaging to a 3PL is cheaper |
| Vendors are readily available | Easy to deploy quickly |

**Example:** A jewellery firm's core competency is design, but machining requires practical skill. Outsource manufacturing, retain design in-house.

<Aside type="note">
Effective outsourcing is critical to strong, resilient supply chains. Firms rarely 'do it all' anymore. Core competency = integrating the right partners to deliver value.
</Aside>

## Supply Portfolio & Kraljic Matrix

### What is a Supply Portfolio?

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** The amalgamation of all products and services that a firm procures.

Not about vendors — about the *items/services procured*. The firm combines these to create its final product/service for the end consumer.
</Aside>

Analysing the portfolio → assess as-is procurement strategy → define future strategy.

### Kraljic Matrix — Framework Overview

<Aside type="note">
**Proposed by:** Peter Kraljic

**Published:** "Purchasing Must Become Supply Management" — Harvard Business Review, 1983

**Type:** Framework (not a theory) — widely used in SCM; variants exist in demand-side management too.

**Purpose:** Compare effort/risk/importance across items in the supply portfolio.
</Aside>

### Two Dimensions of Segmentation

| Dimension | What It Measures | Tools |
|---|---|---|
| **Profit Impact** | How much does procuring this item affect firm profitability? Measured by % share of total purchase value and perceived impact on profitability. | Spend analysis, ABC analysis, VED analysis |
| **Supply Risk** | How risky is supply of this item? Factors: supplier location (farther = higher risk), number of suppliers (more = lower risk), perishability (higher = higher risk) | Risk assessment frameworks |

### Quadrant Summary (Preview)

| Quadrant | Supply Risk | Profit Impact | Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Non-Critical | Low | Low | Streamline, automate |
| Leverage | Low | High | Negotiate hard, competitive bidding |
| Strategic | High | High | Long-term partnerships, deep collaboration |
| Bottleneck | High | Low | Ensure availability, safety stock, close monitoring |
