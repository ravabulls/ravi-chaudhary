## Central Question: What is the Right SC for a Given Product?

Two things must be understood:
1. Type/nature of product flowing through the SC
2. Priority/competitive strategy of the SC

**Product type + SC priority → SC strategy (efficient or responsive)**

The framework used: **Functional vs. Innovative Products**

---

## Product Segmentation: Functional vs. Innovative

### Functional Products

<Aside type="note">
**Characteristics:** Stable, constant, low-uncertainty demand. Long product life cycle. Focus: cost minimization.
</Aside>

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| Demand | Stable, predictable |
| Product Life Cycle | Long |
| Lead Time | Longer (consolidation and cost focus) |
| Stockout Rate | Low |
| Product Variety | Low |
| SC Focus | Cost minimization |
| SC Type | Efficient |

**Examples:** Steel (stable demand, long life cycle), everyday household products.

### Innovative Products

<Aside type="note">
**Characteristics:** Volatile, uncertain demand. Short product life cycle. Focus: flexibility and responsiveness.
</Aside>

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| Demand | Volatile, uncertain |
| Product Life Cycle | Short |
| Lead Time | Short (must be available as demand arises) |
| Stockout Rate | High |
| Product Variety | High |
| SC Focus | Flexibility and responsiveness |
| SC Type | Responsive |

**Examples:** Mobile phones (frequent new models, shrinking time gap between launches), consumer electronics with rapid tech changes.

### Quick Comparison

| | Functional (F) | Innovative (I) |
|---|---|---|
| Demand | Stable | Volatile |
| Life Cycle | Long | Short |
| Lead Time | Long | Short |
| Stockout Risk | Low | High |
| Product Variety | Low | High |
| SC Focus | Cost | Flexibility |
| SC Type | Efficient | Responsive |
| Strategy | Push | Pull |

<Aside type="note">
**Key match:** Functional → Efficient SC → Push | Innovative → Responsive SC → Pull
</Aside>

---

## Push Strategy

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** All SC processes are forecast-driven. Goods are produced and pushed through the SC based on demand predictions.

**Flow:** Production → Distribution → Retail (all based on forecast)
</Aside>

**Characteristics:**
- Forecast-driven decisions
- High volume / Mass production
- Inventory accumulation — stock built in anticipation of demand
- Low customization — standard products
- High initial investment

**Challenges:**
- Limited scope to handle demand variability
- If product not sold → carried for long → inventory obsolescence risk
- Low responsiveness to sudden demand changes

**Best used when:** demand is stable and predictable, product is functional with a long life cycle, and the goal is cost efficiency.

---

## Pull Strategy

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** All SC functions (production + distribution) are driven by actual customer demand. Goods are manufactured only after demand is received.

**Flow:** Customer demand → Production → Distribution
</Aside>

**Characteristics:**
- Demand-driven decisions
- High customization — responds to specific customer needs
- Reduced inventory
- High responsiveness
- Low obsolescence risk — no excess inventory built up
- Efficient resource utilization

**Challenges:**
- Lead time management — critical and difficult
- Coordination complexity
- Uncertain demand
- Production capacity may limit response

**Best used when:** demand is uncertain/volatile, product is innovative with short life cycle, and the goal is flexibility and responsiveness.

---

## Push-Pull Combination (Hybrid Strategy)

### Reality in Practice

In practice, pure push or pure pull rarely exists. Most real SCs use a **combination of both**, separated by a **Push-Pull Boundary**.

<Aside type="note">
**Push-Pull Boundary:** The point in the SC that separates the push zone from the pull zone.

- **Zone 1 (upstream):** Push — low uncertainty
- **Zone 2 (downstream):** Pull — high uncertainty / customization

Position of boundary depends on the **level of customization required**.
</Aside>

- Boundary **closer to customer** → less complexity in SC
- Boundary **closer to supplier** → more customization → higher complexity

---

## Push-Pull Boundary — Examples

### Example 1: Vehicle Color Customization

**Setup:** Same vehicle type offered in different colors depending on customer choice.

| Zone | Players | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Push Zone** | Supplier → Manufacturer → Distributor | Vehicle type is standard; assembly line runs on forecast |
| **Boundary** | At the Distributor | Customer communicates color/accessory preferences here |
| **Pull Zone** | Distributor → Customer | Distributor customizes (paints, adds components) per customer demand |

<Aside type="note">
Standardize upstream (push) → Customize downstream (pull). Boundary = at distributor.
</Aside>

### Example 2: Component-Level Customization (Automobiles)

**Setup:** Different vehicle variants customized per customer order at the component/raw material level.

| Zone | Players | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| **Pull Zone** | Suppliers | Deliver specific components per customer order |
| **Boundary** | Between Supplier and Manufacturing | — |
| **Push Zone** | Manufacturing → Distributor → Customer | Once components received, assembly is standard; push to customer |

<Aside type="note">
Pull at supplier level (component variability), Push post-manufacturing (standard assembly + delivery). Boundary = between supplier and manufacturing.
</Aside>

## Managing the Push-Pull Boundary

### Buffer Inventory
Carry buffer inventory at the players on either side of the push-pull boundary. Buffer absorbs demand fluctuations and enables customization without disrupting upstream.

### Postponement Strategy
<Aside type="note">
**Postponement** = delay customization/differentiation as long as possible in the SC.

Standard product moved upstream via push → customization done at the last possible point (pull zone).

**Ensures:** flexibility while maintaining cost efficiency upstream.
</Aside>
