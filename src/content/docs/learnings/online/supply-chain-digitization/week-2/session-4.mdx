## Part 1: Analytical Product Segmentation — Demand Variability & Weekly Sales

### Case Setup

- **Company:** Electronic gadgets manufacturer (smartphones, tablets, smart watches, other gadgets)
- **Operations:** 2 cities — City A and City B; 2 channels — Online and Retail
- **Data:** Weekly sales for 20 SKUs over 8 weeks
- **Segmentation variables:** Average Weekly Sales (volume indicator) + Demand Variability (stability indicator)

---

### Measuring Demand Variability — Coefficient of Variation (CoV)

<Aside type="note">
**Why CoV and not just Standard Deviation?**

- Standard Deviation (σ): measures *absolute* variability — fluctuation level
- CoV (σ/μ): measures variability *relative to average demand* — normalizes across SKUs with different demand levels

CoV allows fair comparison across SKUs. This is the major advantage.
</Aside>

| Formula | Description |
|---|---|
| **CoV = σ / μ** | Coefficient of Variation |
| **μ = Σxᵢ / n** | Mean demand (sum of orders ÷ number of observations) |
| **σ = √[Σ(xᵢ − x̄)² / n]** | Standard deviation |

- **CoV = 0.1** → very stable demand
- **CoV = 0.5+** → high variability

---

### Steps to Calculate & Segment (Excel-based)

<Steps>
1. Collect 8-week weekly demand data for all 20 SKUs for City A and City B separately.
2. Calculate SKU-wise average demand (using AVERAGEIFS formula) for each city.
3. Calculate SKU-wise standard deviation (using STDEVIFS formula) for each city.
4. Compute CoV = σ / μ for each SKU per city.
5. Plot a Scatter Plot — X-axis: CoV (demand variability), Y-axis: Average weekly sales.
6. Combine City A + City B demand → compute joint average demand + joint σ + joint CoV.
7. Plot combined scatter for the Distribution Center view (serving both cities).
8. Build the 4-Quadrant Matrix to assign SC strategy to each product group.
</Steps>

<Aside type="note">
The combined (joint) CoV represents the variability faced by the distribution center serving both cities.
</Aside>

**Key observation from scatter plot:** The same SKU may have different CoV in different cities — demand pattern is city-specific.

### 4-Quadrant Strategy Matrix

| Quadrant | CoV | Avg Weekly Sales | Product Type | SC Type | Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Q1 (Top-Left)** | Low | High | Essential / High-volume | Efficient | **Push** |
| **Q2 (Bottom-Right)** | High | Low | Customized / Niche | Responsive | **Pull** |
| **Q3 (Top-Right)** | High | High | Consumer electronics / High-tech | Hybrid | **Push-Pull** |
| **Q4 (Bottom-Left)** | Low | Low | Basic / Slow-moving | Hybrid | **Push-Pull** |

**Examples by quadrant:**
- **Q1:** Standard commodity goods, staple consumer products
- **Q2:** Special edition collectibles, highly customized products
- **Q3:** Consumer electronics, tech gadgets with rapid model changes
- **Q4:** Non-seasonal apparel basics, basic electronic components

<Aside type="note">
Quick recall: Q1 = Push (low CoV, high sales). Q2 = Pull (high CoV, low sales). Q3 & Q4 = Hybrid Push-Pull.
</Aside>

## Part 2: Kraljic Matrix — Procurement/Sourcing Segmentation

<Aside type="note">
**Framework by:** Peter Kraljic

**Purpose:** Classify components/inputs required for manufacturing based on two factors. Helps decide supplier relationship strategy and procurement approach for each component type.
</Aside>

### Two Axes

| Axis | What It Measures | Factors |
|---|---|---|
| **X-axis — Supply Risk** | How risky is supply of this item? | Availability, number of suppliers, competitive demand, make-or-buy feasibility |
| **Y-axis — Profit Impact** | How much does this item affect firm profitability? | Volume required, % of total purchase cost, direct/indirect impact on growth |

---

### 4 Quadrants of the Kraljic Matrix

#### 1. Non-Critical Items *(Low Supply Risk + Low Profit Impact)*

- Product is highly standardized; abundant supply — many suppliers available
- Easy to manage — multiple sourcing options exist
- **Strategy:** Streamline procurement, reduce admin effort, automate ordering

#### 2. Leverage Items *(Low Supply Risk + High Profit Impact)*

- Abundant supply, but high impact on profit
- Company has strong purchasing power — can negotiate hard
- **Strategy:** Exploit full purchasing power — competitive bidding, negotiate pricing aggressively
- Requires: good pricing strategy

#### 3. Strategic Items *(High Supply Risk + High Profit Impact)*

- Most critical category — high risk AND high profit impact
- Affects business on a long-term basis
- Cannot switch suppliers easily — partnership approach required
- **Strategy:** Long-term supplier relationships + deep collaboration; supply continuity, joint development, strategic alliances

#### 4. Bottleneck Items *(High Supply Risk + Low Profit Impact)*

- High supply risk, but minimal profit impact if unavailable
- Unavailability can still disrupt operations even if profit impact is low
- Very low control over suppliers — difficult to manage
- **Strategy:** Ensure availability through safety stock / alternate sourcing; monitor closely

---

### Kraljic Matrix — Quick Reference

| Quadrant | Supply Risk | Profit Impact | Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| Non-Critical | Low | Low | Streamline, automate |
| Leverage | Low | High | Negotiate hard, competitive bidding |
| Strategic | High | High | Long-term partnerships, collaborate |
| Bottleneck | High | Low | Secure supply, hold safety stock |
