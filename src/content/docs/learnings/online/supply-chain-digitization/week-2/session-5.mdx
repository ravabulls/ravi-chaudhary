## What is Inventory Segmentation?

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** Categorizing a company's inventory into distinct groups based on defined criteria. Each group gets a tailored management strategy.
</Aside>

**Key Benefits:**
- Cost optimization of the supply chain
- Improved service levels for each SKU category
- Right storage system decided per inventory type
- Better picking strategy in warehouse/DC based on frequency and characteristics
- Improved overall operational performance

---

## Popular Inventory Segmentation Methods

### 1. ABC Analysis *(Revenue-based)*

Classifies inventory by revenue contribution — the Pareto principle applied to inventory.

| Segment | % of Products | % of Revenue |
|---|---|---|
| **A** | ~20% | ~80% |
| **B** | ~30% | ~15% |
| **C** | ~50% | ~5% |

<Aside type="note">
20% of SKUs drive 80% of revenue. Focus management effort on A items.
</Aside>

### 2. FSN Analysis *(Movement-based)*

Classifies inventory by speed of movement/consumption rate.

| Category | % of Items | Warehouse Stay | Characteristic |
|---|---|---|---|
| **F — Fast Moving** | ~10% | Very short (< 10% of avg cumulative stay) | High consumption rate |
| **S — Slow Moving** | ~30% | Moderate | Stays longer, ~20% of avg cumulative stay |
| **N — Non Moving** | ~50% | Stagnant | No consumption for extended period; turnover ratio < 1 |

### 3. VED Analysis *(Criticality-based)*

Classifies inventory by criticality to business operations.

| Category | Meaning |
|---|---|
| **V — Vital** | Absolutely crucial — business cannot function without these |
| **E — Essential** | Very important, next in priority after Vital |
| **D — Desirable** | Not strictly necessary but still needed for smooth operations |

<Aside type="note">
VED is common in manufacturing, hospitals, and defense — wherever certain items are mission-critical.
</Aside>

### 4. XYZ Analysis *(Demand Variability-based)*

Classifies inventory based on predictability of demand.

| Category | Demand Variability |
|---|---|
| **X** | Little or no variation — highly predictable |
| **Y** | Unsteady demand — but can be predicted to a certain extent |
| **Z** | Very high variation — no trend or predictable causal factors; hardest to manage |

---

## Limitation of Traditional Methods

All four methods above are **single-criterion** approaches. Real business decisions require multiple criteria simultaneously. Single-criterion methods → oversimplification → suboptimal strategies.

---

## Advanced Inventory Segmentation Approaches

| Method | Description |
|---|---|
| **Mathematical Programming** | LP or NLP — formulate segmentation as optimization problem considering multiple criteria |
| **Metaheuristics** | Genetic Algorithm (GA), Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO), Simulated Annealing — used when problem is too complex for exact methods |
| **AI / Machine Learning** | ANN, SVM, Back Propagation, KNN, Regression — heavily data-driven |
| **MCDM** (Multi-Criteria Decision Making) | AHP, Fuzzy AHP, ANP — incorporates expert opinion to weigh multiple factors |
| **Hybrid Approaches** | Combinations (e.g., AHP + ML, GA + LP) — used when no single method is adequate |

<Aside type="tip">
**For exams:** Know the 4 traditional methods well. For advanced methods — be able to name them and state why they are used (multi-criteria, data availability, complexity).
</Aside>

## AHP — Analytical Hierarchy Process

<Aside type="note">
**Proposed by:** Thomas L. Saaty

**Type:** Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM) method

**Applications:** Supplier selection, procurement, product/service design, resource allocation, inventory segmentation.

**Core idea:** Organizes complex decisions into a hierarchical structure. Does pairwise comparison of all factors to determine relative importance.
</Aside>

### Saaty Scale (1–9)

| Score | Meaning |
|---|---|
| 1 | Equally important |
| 3 | Moderately more important |
| 5 | Strongly more important |
| 7 | Very strongly more important |
| 9 | Extremely more important |
| 2, 4, 6, 8 | Intermediate values |

### AHP Steps

<Steps>
1. Define problem and state objective clearly.
2. Develop hierarchical framework — list all criteria and their relationships.
3. Construct Pairwise Comparison Matrix for each level using the Saaty scale.
4. Normalize the matrix — calculate criterion weights (priority vector).
5. Calculate Consistency Ratio (CR) = CI / RI (CI = Consistency Index, RI = Random Index from standard table based on matrix size).
6. Check CR: if CR < 0.1 → consistent → use weights. If CR ≥ 0.1 → inconsistent → return to Step 3 and redo pairwise comparison.
</Steps>

<Aside type="caution">
**CR < 0.1 is the MUST-PASS condition in AHP.** If not satisfied, the expert input is considered unreliable — redo the comparison.
</Aside>

## AHP Case: Inventory Segmentation — XYZ E-tailer

### Case Setup

- **Company:** XYZ E-tail — e-commerce retailer with a fixed 3-year warehouse space agreement
- **Policy:** Product listed as "available" only if physically present in warehouse
- **Challenge:** Management wants to expand product portfolio but warehouse space is limited
- **Previous service level:** 95% for all SKUs
- **New plan:** Classify 25 SKUs into 3 groups with different service levels

| Class | Service Level |
|---|---|
| A | 95% |
| B | 90% |
| C | 85% |

- **Tool used:** AHP to classify all 25 SKUs

### 6 Criteria Considered

| Criterion | Unit | Direction |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly Demand | Units/month | Higher = better |
| Priority of Product Category | Score 1–10 | Higher = better |
| Supplier Reliability | % of perfect orders | Higher = better |
| Profit Margin | % | Higher = better |
| Lead Time | Hours | **Lower = better** |
| Likelihood of Return | % | **Lower = better** |

<Aside type="note">
Factors have different units AND different directions (some higher-better, some lower-better). Normalization is required before scoring.
</Aside>

### AHP Calculation Steps Applied to Case

<Steps>
1. Fill pairwise comparison matrix using Saaty scale for all 6 criteria (provided by domain experts). Example: Monthly demand rated 3× more important than Priority of product category.
2. Normalize matrix → calculate criterion weights for all 6 factors.
3. Check consistency → CR = 0.08 < 0.1 → Consistent ✓
</Steps>

### Normalizing SKU Data

Since criteria are in different units, normalize all to a 0–1 scale.

| Criterion Type | Formula |
|---|---|
| **Higher-is-better** | (xᵢ − min) / (max − min) |
| **Lower-is-better** (Lead Time, Return %) | 1 − [(xᵢ − min) / (max − min)] |

This converts "lower is better" into "higher is better" — consistent direction for all criteria.

### Calculating SKU Score

For each SKU:

> **SKU Score = Σ (AHP Weight_i × Normalized Score_i)**

Higher score = more important SKU overall.

### ABC Classification from SKU Scores

<Steps>
1. Sort all 25 SKUs by Combined SKU Score in descending order.
2. Calculate cumulative score and cumulative % of total score.
3. Apply cut-offs to assign classes.
</Steps>

| Class | Cumulative Score Cut-off | Items (of 25) | Service Level |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | Top ~60% | 7 items | 95% |
| B | Next ~25% | 8 items | 90% |
| C | Remaining ~15% | 10 items | 85% |

<Aside type="note">
Cut-off percentages (60/25/15) are **decision-based** — set by the company as per business strategy. These are not fixed universal values.
</Aside>
