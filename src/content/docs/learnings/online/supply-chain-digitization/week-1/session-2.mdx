## 1. SC Stakeholders & Their Roles

Not all stakeholders are present in every SC — depends on SC type.

| Stakeholder | Role |
|---|---|
| Suppliers | Provide/sell RM or components needed for final product |
| Manufacturers | Convert RM → finished goods (various production processes) |
| Transporters (Logistics) | Move RM supplier → manufacturer; move FG → distribution network |
| Distributors | Store RM and FG; release to next stage based on production schedules/demand |
| Retailers | Closest to customer; sell FG via online or physical stores |
| Consumers | End users — this is where the SC terminates |

---

## 2. Forward SC vs. Reverse SC

**Forward SC:** Supplier → Manufacturer → Distributor → Retailer → Customer

**Reverse SC:** Customer returns product → flows backward through the chain

| Return Type | Handled By |
|---|---|
| Minor repair | Retailer or distributor level → product returned to customer |
| Major repair / exchange | Product goes back to manufacturer |

<Aside type="caution">
Both forward and reverse SC must be managed — supply chains must now be viewed with 'new lenses'.
</Aside>

## 3. Value Chain vs. Supply Chain

Often confused — same processes, very different focus.

### Value Chain

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** Focus on adding value at each step — planning, design, development, delivery.
</Aside>

- **Objective:** enhance customer experience → justify premium pricing
- **Output:** competitive advantage — helps company perform better than rivals

**Example — Coffee Shop:**
- Source high-quality beans from renowned region
- In-house roasting to preserve quality
- Welcoming store ambience and comfortable seating
- Personalized service and customization options
- Result: every step adds value → customer pays premium price

### Supply Chain

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** Focus on efficient movement and delivery of goods from supplier to end customer.
</Aside>

- **Objective:** ensure product reaches customer — right manner, right time, customer satisfaction

**Example — Smartphone:**
- Source components (microchips, screens, batteries) from global suppliers
- Transport to assembly plants (multiple locations)
- Assemble into finished smartphones
- Distribute to global retailer stores → fulfill customer demand

**Summary:** Value Chain = WHY value is added (competitive edge) | Supply Chain = HOW goods move efficiently

## 4. Competitive Strategy

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** Searching for a favourable competitive position in an industry; achieving a profitable and sustainable position against market forces (strong brand recognition, economies of scale, etc.).
</Aside>

| Company Type | Strategy | How |
|---|---|---|
| Retail chain store | Cost Leadership | Large volume, wide product range at LOW cost |
| Smartphone brands | Differentiation | Frequent new models; short gap between launches keeps customers hooked |
| Hypermarkets | Volume + Low Price | All products under one roof; high volume → low pricing → high footfall |

<Aside type="caution">
Just as SC ≠ same for all, strategy ≠ same for all companies — depends on the competitive position they want in the market.
</Aside>

---

## 5. Competitive Priorities

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** Critical operational dimensions a process/SC must possess to satisfy customers. They drive decisions on capacity, technology, production process, planning, and control.
</Aside>

A company does NOT need all five — select the most relevant.

| Priority | Description |
|---|---|
| **Cost** | Minimise cost at every step of SC/manufacturing → enables low pricing |
| **Quality** | All products and processes comply with specifications |
| **Flexibility** | Ability to offer varying volumes AND varying product mix |
| **Delivery** | Reliable and quick delivery; stock availability when demand arises |
| **Innovativeness** | Speed of new product introduction → low time-to-market |

---

## 6. Manufacturing Processes (5 Types)

Competitive priorities determine which manufacturing process to select.

| Process | Volume | Variety | Flow | Example |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **1. Project** | Very low | Very high | Stationary | Construction, ships, aircraft |
| **2. Job Shop** | Low-medium | High | Jumbled | Custom furniture workshop |
| **3. Batch Production** | Medium | Medium | Disconnected line | Baking |
| **4. Assembly Line** | High | Low | Sequential | Automobile manufacturing |
| **5. Continuous Process** | Very high | Very low | Continuous | Petroleum refining |

**Rule of thumb:** Moving from Project → Continuous = increasing standardisation, increasing volume, decreasing variety.

---

## 7. Linking It All Together

**Competitive Strategy → Competitive Priorities → Manufacturing Process → SC Type**

| SC Type | Description | Linked Process |
|---|---|---|
| **Responsive SC** | Product reaches customer fast, per customer requirement | Job Shop / Batch (high-variety) |
| **Efficient SC** | Deliver at minimum cost | Assembly / Continuous (high-volume, standardised) |

<Aside type="tip">
**Exam angle:** "How does competitive strategy translate into SC design?" → Strategy → Priorities (cost/quality/flexibility/delivery/innovativeness) → Manufacturing Process → Responsive or Efficient SC. Use examples from this session.
</Aside>
