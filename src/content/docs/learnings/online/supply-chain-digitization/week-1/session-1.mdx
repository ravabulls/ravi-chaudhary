## 1. What is a Supply Chain?

<Aside type="note">
**Definition:** An interconnected journey of raw materials, components, and finished goods — from origin (before their assembly) to sale to the customer.
</Aside>

The word **"chain"** = a series of links, where each link = a player/entity in the SC.

**Purpose:** Ensure products are manufactured and reach the final customer — right time, right quantity, right quality.

### Key Players (in order)

**Raw Material Supplier → Manufacturer → Warehouse → Distributor → Retailer → Customer**

Product moves in different forms and shapes at each stage.

| Player | Role |
|---|---|
| RM Supplier | Deliver raw material — right time, qty, quality |
| Manufacturer | Convert RM → finished goods |
| Warehouse / Distributor / Retailer | Move product closer to end customer |

<Aside type="caution">
Not all supply chains are the same — structure depends on product type and business model.
</Aside>

## 2. Types of Supply Chains — Examples

### (a) Automobile SC

- Complex, multi-tier SC with 1,000+ raw material suppliers
- **Flow:** RM Suppliers → Sub-component Mfg → Interior/Powertrain Assemblers → Final Assembly → Dealers → Consumers
- Sub-components: dashboards, transmission, engine (each needs dedicated suppliers)
- Final assembly: ~1,000 unique components assembled at this stage
- Distribution: can have multi-layer distribution centres between assembler and dealers depending on geography

### (b) Pharmaceutical SC

- Two key input types: (1) Raw Materials (RM) + (2) Packaging Materials (PM)
- **Flow:** RM/PM Suppliers → Manufacturers → Central Warehouse → Distributors/Stockists/Semi-Wholesalers → Retailers/Pharmacies/Hospitals → Consumers
- **Critical triad:** RM Supplier ↔ Manufacturer ↔ R&D (R&D drives updated product requirements → feeds back to suppliers)
- Inbound logistics via rail and road; exports handled by export agents and C&F agents
- Network is complex — necessary to reach patients at every last-mile point

### (c) Oil & Gas SC

- **Flow:** Exploration → Production → Crude Oil Storage → Refinery (distillation etc.) → Storage Terminals → Retail/Industrial/Commercial Markets
- Distinctive feature: transportation mode changes at each stage

| Stage | Transport Mode |
|---|---|
| Exploration → Production | Pipeline or ship |
| Refinery → Storage Terminals | Pipeline or ship |
| Storage Terminals → End Markets | Pipeline / Rail / Road / Tanker |

Processing method and storage format also change across stages.

### (d) E-Commerce SC

- **Flow:** Customer Order → 3PL Central Warehouse → Supplier → 3PL Central Warehouse → 3PL Local Warehouse → Last-Mile Delivery → Customer
- **Two parallel flows:**
  - **Information flow:** order details — product type, qty, price
  - **Physical flow:** physical movement of goods
- Key difference vs. traditional SC: no manufacturer or RM supplier visible — only product suppliers and 3PL
- If you trace the supplier's SC, manufacturers and RM suppliers reappear — just outside the e-commerce perspective

## 3. Key Concepts to Remember

- SC structure ≠ fixed — depends on product type, business model, and geography
- Not every player is present in every SC (e.g., no manufacturer in e-commerce SC)
- Number of tiers/layers directly impacts complexity and coordination effort
- Good exercise: pick any product → trace its SC → identify each player and their role

### Quick Comparison

| SC Type | Key Feature |
|---|---|
| Automotive | Most tiers, 1,000+ suppliers, highest assembly complexity |
| Pharma | R&D plays active role, last-mile reach critical, complex distribution web |
| Oil & Gas | Unique transport modes (pipeline/ship), product transforms at refinery |
| E-Commerce | Info flow as important as physical flow, 3PL central, no in-SC manufacturing |

**Exam angle:** "Explain with examples why all supply chains are not the same." — Use 2–3 examples above, highlighting: players involved, transport modes, product transformation, and information flow.
</Aside>
