2.  Warehouse Management System (WMS)
Why WMS is a Separate System from ERP
Warehouse activities are very specific to the product type, facility layout, and process — not common across all businesses
ERP handles common business functions (finance, HR, projects) — not granular warehouse ops
Similar reason applies to manufacturing (MES) — ERP doesn't handle shop floor specifics
WMS and ERP are often from the same vendor but are separate modules — integration is required

Warehouse Facility Types WMS Covers
Warehouse: long-term storage — items may stay for months before moving
Distribution Center (DC): shorter hold — picking and dispatching to retailers regularly
Fulfillment Center: e-commerce focused — fast individual order fulfillment
Sort Center / Cross-dock: items arrive and leave within 8–12 hours — no long-term storage

Warehouse Layout — Key Terminology (from Diagram)
Pallet positions: individual storage slots (each square = 1 pallet from top view). Can be stacked vertically on racks.
Aisle space: movement corridor between storage rows — no storage, only transit
Inbound docks: area where incoming vehicles unload materials (could have multiple docks)
Outbound dock: area where outgoing vehicles load materials for dispatch
Yard: open space around building — used for vehicle parking and large equipment
SKU: Stock Keeping Unit — a specific product type + size + unit (e.g., shampoo 200ml, detergent 5kg)

Core Warehouse Activity Sequence

1
Receiving (Inbound)
Materials arrive at inbound docks. Items unloaded, scanned (T&T integration), GRN generated. Dock is checked for completion.
2
Put Away
Items moved from inbound to designated storage location. WMS provides sequencing of put away to minimize travel. Location assigned based on SKU movement speed, size, etc.
3
Storage
Items held in warehouse for planned duration. WMS shows real-time pallet position availability. Tracks utilization by zone, rack, position.
4
Picking
Orders received → WMS generates pick list (sequence + quantity). Picker or automated equipment follows list. Picking order minimizes travel distance.
5
Staging & Dispatch
Picked items moved to outbound area (staging). Loaded onto vehicle. GRN-equivalent for outbound generated. Dock allocated by TMS.

3.  WMS — Key Functionalities
WMS Functionality
What it Does
Real-time Visualization
Shows top-view (and other views) of warehouse — which pallet positions are full, empty, partially empty
Color-coded by status: fully occupied, partially occupied, fully free
Gives instant snapshot of capacity utilization
Space Allocation / Slotting
Decision support: where to assign SKUs based on movement speed (fast-movers near outbound), size (large items at back), product type
Suggests optimal pallet position for each incoming product
Goods Receipt Management
Records inbound completion — all pallets unloaded? GRN generated? Integrated with T&T scanning
Links to ERP materials module
Put Away Sequencing
Tells picker/operator: visit position A first, then B — minimizes travel distance
Also handles multi-pallet put away sequencing
Pick List Generation
Based on orders received — generates ordered pick list with quantities and locations
Assigns pickers (personnel) and equipment to pick sequence
Minimizes travel; can handle partial picks and bulk picks
Performance Reporting
Daily productivity metrics: throughput, utilization %, equipment utilization, picker productivity
Zone-level utilization, facility-level throughput
Supports operations review and process improvement

★  WMS = real-time inventory visibility + activity sequencing + performance reporting. It tells you WHERE things are and WHAT to do next.

4.  Transportation Management System (TMS)
What TMS Focuses On
TMS manages the movement of materials between facilities — factory → warehouse → DC → retail store
Covers: manpower, equipment, commercial terms, regulatory compliance, route planning
Overlaps with ERP (vendor management, invoicing) and WMS (dock allocation) — integration is key
Can be implemented for all SC movements or only specific segments (e.g., last-mile only)

TMS Key Terminology
FTL — Full Truck Load: vehicle used to full capacity for one consignment
LTL — Less than Truck Load: vehicle carries partial loads — may combine multiple consignments
Backhaul: return journey of vehicle after delivery — goes to warehouse OR proceeds to another pickup
Dock: loading/unloading bay at a warehouse — same term used in warehousing and port contexts
Yard management: managing vehicle and equipment parking area around the warehouse building

TMS Functionalities

TMS Functionality
What it Does
Dock Scheduling
Which vehicle goes to which inbound/outbound dock? How many docks should operate today?
When will a dock be free? → Assign next vehicle. When should dispatch be scheduled by?
Connects TMS with WMS — dock availability depends on warehouse pick/load progress
Yard Management
Manages open space around the warehouse — parking of vehicles awaiting dock allocation
Positioning of heavy equipment (cranes, forklifts) awaiting call
Tracks what is in the yard and when it needs to move
Freight Management
Mode selection: road vs sea vs air — decision support based on cost, speed, urgency
FTL (Full Truck Load) vs LTL (Less than Truck Load) decisions — maximize vehicle utilization
Compares freight costs across options for a given route
Documentation
Auto-generates shipping documents, delivery orders, customs paperwork
Handles regulatory/compliance requirements for inter-state or international shipments
Reduces manual documentation work on a per-trip basis
Vehicle Routing
For last-mile / multi-stop deliveries: decides optimal order of retail stores to visit
Minimizes total distance / time for a given load
Also determines backhaul: should empty vehicle return to warehouse or proceed to another pick-up?

★  TMS = decision support for MOVEMENT. It answers: which mode, which route, which dock, which vehicle, and when. Eliminates manual route planning and documentation.

5.  Integration — How ERP, WMS & TMS Connect
All three systems are interrelated — data flows between them continuously:

Integration
Data Flow & Purpose
ERP ↔ WMS
Production schedule informs warehouse of expected inbound. Dispatch records update ERP materials module. Spend data flows to ERP finance.
ERP ↔ TMS
Vendor delivery schedules from ERP procurement module inform TMS dock scheduling. Freight costs flow back to ERP for spend analysis.
WMS ↔ TMS
WMS tells TMS when dock is clear and items are ready for loading. TMS tells WMS vehicle arrival time so dock can be pre-cleared.
WMS ↔ T&T
Barcode / RFID scans at inbound dock update WMS inventory in real time. Pick scan confirms what has been picked and loaded.
TMS ↔ T&T
Vehicle tracking (GPS, telematics) updates TMS with live vehicle location and delivery confirmation at customer point.

Example end-to-end flow: production completes at factory (ERP) → truck dispatched (TMS) → arrives at dock (WMS dock scheduling) → items scanned in (T&T → WMS) → pick list generated for retail orders (WMS) → vehicle routed to stores (TMS)

6.  Implementation Considerations for WMS & TMS
Same principles as ERP — must evaluate need, cost-benefit, and fit before procuring:

Implementation Question
Why it Matters
Scale of business?
Determines which modules to implement and whether to procure or build in-house
ERP integration possible?
If WMS/TMS data cannot feed into ERP, value of the system is severely reduced — data must be reportable and accountable
Partner WMS/TMS fit?
Can your warehouse WMS talk to your vendor's or client's TMS? Cross-system compatibility is key for end-to-end visibility
Digital readiness of people?
Staff must be trained on devices, software, tools — not just operational tasks. Traditional shop-floor-only thinking is insufficient
Adaptability to change?
Product mix changes, demand variability, regulation changes (taxes, pricing) — can the WMS/TMS adapt without full redesign?
Routing complexity?
Complex multi-stop or multi-mode routing requirements determine how sophisticated a TMS module is needed
Scalability?
Ability to adapt quickly also determines how fast the business can scale. Rigid systems slow growth.

India context: massive warehousing infrastructure development underway — WMS adoption accelerating. PM Gati Shakti initiative driving logistics digitization.
Insight: shop floor warehouse staff must be digitally upskilled — purely operational training is no longer sufficient

Session Summary
WMS: manages activities inside the warehouse — receiving, put away, storage, picking, dispatch, performance metrics
TMS: manages material movement between facilities — dock scheduling, yard mgmt, freight, routing, documentation
Key terms: dock, aisle, pallet position, SKU, FTL, LTL, backhaul, yard, cross-dock, sort center
WMS functionalities: real-time visualization, slotting, GRN, put away sequencing, pick list, performance reporting
TMS functionalities: dock scheduling, yard management, freight mode selection, documentation, vehicle routing
Integration: ERP ↔ WMS ↔ TMS ↔ T&T — all linked. Data flows across all systems in a well-designed SC IS landscape
WMS vs ERP: separate because warehouse activities are too specific and granular for generic ERP — same vendor possible but different modules
Implementation: scale, ERP integration, partner compatibility, digital readiness, adaptability, routing complexity

★ Exam Tip:  WMS ≠ ERP. ERP handles common business functions. WMS handles warehouse-specific activities. They integrate but are separate modules — often from the same or different vendors.
★ Exam Tip:  5 warehouse activities in order: Receiving → Put Away → Storage → Picking → Dispatch (staging). WMS provides decision support at every step.
★ Exam Tip:  TMS key decisions: which dock, which mode (FTL/LTL/road/sea), which route (multi-stop retail delivery), backhaul plan. Each is automated via TMS rather than manual planning.
★ Exam Tip:  Integration matters: WMS dock availability feeds TMS scheduling; TMS vehicle arrival feeds WMS dock prep. T&T scan data feeds WMS inventory. If any system is isolated, its value drops sharply.
