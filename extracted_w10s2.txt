2.  What are Track & Trace Systems?
Technology-enabled systems that record, transmit and store product attributes at each stage of the SC
Used across: sourcing, manufacturing, assembly, dispatch, transportation, last-mile, storage, consumption
Common in: e-commerce (most visible to consumers), brick & mortar retail (scanning at checkout), B2B logistics
Increasingly critical due to: growing e-retail, security & cybersecurity concerns in SC management

Everyday Example — E-commerce Order Tracking
The entire order-to-delivery journey is a live T&T system in action:

1
Order placed
Customer buys on e-commerce app. Order created; product allocated at warehouse.
2
Dispatch notification
Product scanned at origin warehouse. Customer notified: 'dispatched'. App shows product location.
3
In-transit updates
At each transit hub, product is scanned. App updates location chronologically — customer sees full journey.
4
Out for delivery
Local delivery station assigns delivery agent. Customer notified to be available.
5
Last-mile delivery
Delivery agent scans product at customer doorstep. Proof of delivery captured.
6
Delivery confirmation
Customer signs on device OR shares OTP. Triangulation complete — delivery verified.
7
Complaint reference
If quality issue arises, delivery record serves as reference for complaint resolution.

★  T&T systems provide a verifiable, timestamped record of every product event — from dispatch to delivery. This record enables both customer service and complaint resolution.

3.  Key Concept — Triangulation of Information
Triangulation = verifying a transaction using multiple independent data points simultaneously
In e-commerce delivery: delivery agent scan + customer signature/OTP + timestamp + GPS = triangulated proof
Prevents wrongful claims — product, receiver, location and time all recorded independently
Equivalent in B2B SC: GRN (goods received note) + system scan + invoice match = triangulated receipt

★  Triangulation = the reason T&T systems are legally defensible. Any one data point can be disputed; multiple corroborating records together cannot.

4.  Four Major Steps in Any Track & Trace System
All T&T systems — manual or technology-enabled — must cover all four steps:

1
Capture Attributes
Decide which product attributes matter for the application
Examples: serial no., batch no., production location, temperature, humidity, expiry date
Application determines scope — e.g., movement tracking needs serial no. + location + time; cold chain also needs temperature
2
Data Capture & Transmission
How is the attribute data actually collected? — manual (pen/paper) or automated (device-connected)
Manual: prone to fatigue errors; slow for large volumes
Technology: devices capture data and transmit via internet (cloud) OR store locally (temp hard drive) then transfer
3
Data Storage (Management)
Store captured data securely — cloud or local database (not paper — high risk of loss/damage)
Ensure data sanctity: access controls, backup, audit trails
Database must be searchable — e.g., retrieve all records for a specific product 3 weeks ago
4
Data Sharing & Collaboration
Share data with internal stakeholders: shop floor manager, senior management (audit), operations teams
Share selectively with external stakeholders: e.g., 3PL client sees warehouse space/inventory status
Enables real-time visibility and collaborative decision-making across the SC

What Attributes to Capture? — Application Determines Scope
Not all attributes need to be captured for every application — select based on need:
Movement tracking only: serial number + location + timestamp
Cold chain (pharma, food): above + temperature + humidity at each point
Manufacturing component tracking: serial/batch no. + process stage + operator ID
Customer-facing (e-commerce): order ID + transit hub locations + delivery confirmation

6.  Technologies for Track & Trace — Overview
Technologies exist on a spectrum from simple to sophisticated:
Manual labelling → barcode → QR code → RFID → IoT sensors → blockchain (covered progressively)
This session covers: Barcode and QR Code

7.  Barcode Technology
What is a Barcode?
Sticker or label with encoded lines — 1D (vertical lines only) or 2D (vertical + horizontal lines)
Lines represent encoded information — static, embedded at printing time
Information capacity: limited — typically 1–4 attributes (serial no., batch, price, expiry)

How to Capture / Read Barcode Data
Requires: dedicated barcode scanner or handheld device
Distance and angle between scanner and barcode must be precise — slight tilt = scan failure
Product is brought to the scanner (checkout counters) OR scanner is brought to the product (warehouse)

Applications
Retail: checkout counter scanning for price + payment processing (most visible consumer use)
Air cargo / baggage: sticker on luggage encodes flight + destination → scanned at conveyor systems to route correctly
Manufacturing: components scanned one at a time to track usage in processes
Healthcare: patient health records mapped to barcode on wristband or medication
Usually placed on primary packaging (the item itself)

8.  QR Code (Quick Response Code)
What is a QR Code?
An advanced version of barcode — 2D matrix pattern (squares, not just lines)
Can appear on: physical stickers/labels AND in digital formats (screens, presentations, documents)
Information capacity: very high — can link to websites, videos, Drive folders, databases, live data feeds

Static vs Dynamic QR Codes
Static QR: information encoded once at creation — cannot be changed later (like a barcode)
Dynamic QR: content linked to a URL or database — can be updated in real-time without changing the QR code itself
Example: a QR on packaging links to a product page — update the page content anytime; QR stays the same

How to Capture / Read QR Data
Requires: camera-enabled device + scanning app (smartphone, tablet, drone)
More flexible than barcode — tolerates wider angles, greater distances — does not need to be perfectly aligned
Drone application: drone with embedded camera flies over warehouse, scans QR codes on pallets/shelves → hands-free inventory scan

Applications
Secondary packaging: cartons, pallets, outer boxes — covers larger units than barcode
Payments: UPI / merchant QR codes (consumer-facing)
SC traceability: link QR to full product journey, certificates, compliance docs
Drone inventory: automated warehouse scanning without human counting
Software apps: QR as login, verification, or quick-access shortcut within systems
